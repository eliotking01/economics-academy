3.3.3 Economies and Diseconomies of Scale

Edexcel A-Level Economics (9EC0) | Theme 3.3.3

Specification Coverage: Edexcel unit 3.3.3 - Economies and Diseconomies of Scale. Students should be able to understand the shape of the LRAC curve, identify and explain different internal and external economies of scale, analyse the causes of diseconomies of scale, and explain the significance of the minimum efficient scale.

The Long-Run Average Cost Curve

In the long run, firms can change their scale of production. This creates economies and diseconomies of scale, which shape the LRAC curve.

Economies of Scale

Economies of scale are cost advantages that a firm gains by increasing its scale of output. These are internal to the firm and cause LRAC to fall.

Type Explanation Example
Purchasing Bulk-buying discounts on raw materials. A large supermarket chain gets lower prices from suppliers.
Technical Use of more efficient, large-scale machinery or technology. A car factory uses robotic assembly lines.
Financial Easier and cheaper access to finance, such as lower interest rates. A large PLC gets a better loan rate than a small business.
Managerial Specialisation of management, such as separate HR and Finance departments.
Marketing Spreading advertising costs over a larger output. A global brand's television advertising cost per unit sold is very small.
Risk-bearing Diversification across products or markets spreads risk. A conglomerate can absorb losses in one area.

Diseconomies of Scale

Diseconomies of scale are cost disadvantages that arise when a firm becomes too large. These are internal to the firm and cause LRAC to rise.

Type Explanation
Management Bureaucracy, slow decision-making, and loss of control as the hierarchy becomes larger.
Communication Miscommunication and distortion of messages across many layers or departments.
Motivational Workers may feel alienated, causing lower productivity and linking to the principal-agent problem.
Geographical Coordination problems between distant plants or offices.

Minimum Efficient Scale

Minimum Efficient Scale (MES): The lowest point on the LRAC curve.

It is the output level where the firm achieves the lowest possible cost per unit.

Firms operating at or near MES have a significant cost advantage.

External Economies of Scale

External economies of scale are cost advantages that benefit all firms in a growing industry. They are external to the individual firm.

Source Explanation Example
Geographic clusters Ancillary firms locate nearby, reducing transport and logistics costs. Technology firms in Silicon Valley.
Skilled labour pools A concentration of firms attracts specialised workers, reducing training costs. Financial services in London.
Improved infrastructure Transport and communications improve to serve the industry. Road and rail links near a port.
Knowledge spillovers Firms in close proximity share ideas and innovation.

Exam Preparation

  • Define economies and diseconomies of scale clearly.
  • Draw and interpret the LRAC curve, including the sections showing economies and diseconomies of scale and the MES.
  • Identify and explain different internal economies of scale with examples.
  • Explain the causes of diseconomies of scale.
  • Understand external economies of scale and how they differ from internal economies of scale.
