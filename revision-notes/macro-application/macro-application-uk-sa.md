*Comprehensive data and policy examples for exam application*
*Compiled May 2026. Always quote the year alongside the figure in an exam.*

## PART 1: THE UK ECONOMY (Developed Economy)

### 1. Economic Growth

- **Annual real GDP growth: 1.4% in 2025** (ONS, May 2026), following 1.0% in 2024. This is below the long-run trend rate of ~2%.
- **Quarterly growth: +0.6% in Q1 2026** (Jan–Mar), the strongest quarter in a year, after +0.2% in Q4 2025.
- **Monthly growth: +0.3% in March 2026** (ONS).
- **G7 context:** UK GDP in Q4 2025 was 5.3% above its pre-pandemic (Q4 2019) level; Eurozone was 6.7% above; the US has led the G7 at 14.6% above (House of Commons Library).
- **2026 forecast:** OECD cut its UK 2026 growth forecast to 0.8% in April 2026 (from 1.3%) citing the Middle East conflict and energy prices; the IMF also downgraded forecasts following the same shock.
- **Use this for:** Evaluation of macro performance, output gaps, comparisons of recovery speeds, the cost of low productivity growth.

### 2. Unemployment

- **Unemployment rate: 4.9% (Dec 2025–Feb 2026)**, down from 5.2% the previous quarter but **up from 4.4% a year earlier** (ONS).
- **1.78 million people unemployed** — an increase of 206,000 over the year.
- **Employment rate (16–64): 75.0%**.
- **Economic inactivity rate (16–64): 21.0%** — about 9.12 million people, a persistent issue post-COVID (long-term sickness, early retirement).
- **Claimant Count: 1.694 million (March 2026)** — the highest since the pandemic.
- **Causes cited:** weak demand, employer NICs rise (April 2025), tight monetary policy, structural inactivity.

### 3. Youth Unemployment (Aged 16–24)

- **Unemployment rate for 16–24-year-olds: 15.8%** (Dec 2025–Feb 2026), up from 14.6% a year earlier.
- **713,000 young people unemployed**, an increase of 70,000 year on year.
- **Policy example: Labour's "Youth Guarantee"** — launched 2025, offers all 18–21-year-olds access to training, an apprenticeship or help to find work.

### 4. Inflation

- **CPI inflation: 3.3% (March 2026)**, up from 3.0% in February — driven mainly by higher motor fuel prices following the Middle East/Iran conflict (ONS).
- **CPIH: 3.4%**, RPI: 4.3%, **Core CPI (ex food, energy, alcohol, tobacco): 3.1%**.
- **Services inflation: 4.5%** — Bank of England watches this closely as a proxy for domestic price pressure.
- **Goods inflation: 2.1%**.
- **Recent peak:** CPI peaked at **11.1% in October 2022** (41-year high) following the energy price shock.
- **Bank of England forecast (April 2026):** Inflation projected at 3.1% in Q2, 3.3% in Q3 2026, rising further into Q4 because of energy and food prices.
- **Inflation target:** 2% (CPI), set by the government, achieved by the independent Bank of England MPC.

### 5. Current Account & Balance of Payments

- **2025 current account deficit: £74 billion (2.4% of GDP)**, improved from £86 billion (3.0%) in 2024 (ONS/Commons Library).
- **Q4 2025 deficit: £18.4 billion (2.4% of GDP)**, widening after a smaller Q3 deficit.
- **Trade in goods deficit: £243 billion in 2025**, offset by a **trade in services surplus of £205 billion** → overall trade deficit £38 billion.
- **The UK has had a current account deficit every year since 1984** — important "evaluation" point: persistent rather than temporary.
- **Net International Investment Position (end-2025): -£199.8 billion** — net debtor to the world.

### 6. Aggregate Demand Components (2025 shares of GDP, expenditure side)

- **Household consumption: ~58–65% of GDP** (largest component). +0.6% QoQ in Q1 2026.
- **Government consumption: ~20% of GDP**. +0.4% QoQ in Q1 2026.
- **Gross fixed capital formation (investment): ~17% of GDP**. Fell 0.6% in Q1 2026; business investment grew 0.7%.
- **Exports: ~30.6% of GDP** (2025); **Imports: ~31.9% of GDP** → net trade subtracts ~1–2% from GDP.
- **Household saving ratio: 9.9% in Q4 2025** — elevated, reflecting cautious consumers.

### 7. Sector Contributions to GDP (Output side)

- **Services: ~79–80% of GDP**. The single biggest contributor to growth in every recent quarter.
  - Government, education & health (~19% of GDP)
  - Real estate (~12%)
  - Professional, scientific & technical + admin (~12%)
  - Wholesale & retail trade (~11%)
  - **Financial & insurance services (~8%)**
- **Production (industry): ~21% of GDP**, of which manufacturing ~10% and construction ~6%.
- **Agriculture: ~1% of GDP**.
- The UK is increasingly a "post-industrial" economy — useful for essays on deindustrialisation, structural change and the trade deficit in goods.

### 8. Government Budget (Fiscal Position)

- **Public sector net borrowing: £132 billion in 2025/26** (ONS provisional), down £20bn on 2024/25.
- **Borrowing as % of GDP: ~4.4% in FY 2025/26** — still high by historical standards.
- **Tax burden: ~36–37% of GDP** — close to a post-WWII high.
- **2024/25 saw the third-highest borrowing on record** (after 2020/21 pandemic and 2009/10 GFC).
- **Spring Statement 2026** (Chancellor Rachel Reeves) confirmed continued fiscal tightening within Labour's fiscal rules (using PSNFL as the anchor).

### 9. Government Debt

- **Public sector net debt: 93.8% of GDP at end-March 2026** (ONS), up from 93.2% a year earlier.
- **Levels last seen in the early 1960s** — a key talking point for "burden on future generations" essays.
- **Net financial liabilities (PSNFL): 83.3% of GDP** — the government's new fiscal-rule anchor.
- **Debt interest payments: £103 billion in 2024/25** — more than the entire UK education budget. A key opportunity-cost point.

### 10. Monetary Policy

- **Bank Rate: 3.75% (April 2026)** — held since the December 2025 cut from 4.0%.
- **Cycle:** Rates rose from 0.1% (Dec 2021) to a peak of **5.25% (Aug 2023)** to fight inflation, then gradually cut by 1.5 percentage points between August 2024 and December 2025.
- **MPC vote (April 2026): 8–1** to hold, with one member (Huw Pill) voting for a 0.25pp hike.
- **Quantitative Easing/Tightening:** APF peaked at **£895 billion**; reduced to **£527 billion by April 2026** through QT. MPC plans to reduce by a further £70bn in the year to September 2026.
- **2% CPI inflation target**, set by the government, pursued by the independent MPC (since 1997).

### 11. Fiscal Policy — Taxation & Government Spending

- **Total government spending: £1.37 trillion in 2025/26**.
- **Key tax rates (2025/26):**
  - Income tax: basic 20%, higher 40%, additional 45%.
  - VAT: 20% (standard).
  - Corporation tax: 25% (main rate).
  - National Insurance (employer): rate rose from 13.8% to 15% in April 2025, threshold lowered — generated ~£25bn but criticised as a "jobs tax".
- **Labour ruled out raising income tax, NI (employee) and VAT** at the 2024 election — limiting fiscal room.

### 12. Spending on Healthcare (NHS)

- **DHSC total budget 2025/26: £217 billion** (rising to £246bn cash by 2028/29).
- **Real-terms growth: 2.4% p.a.** over 2025/26 to 2028/29 (Health Foundation/NHS Confed).
- **NHS England specifically: 3% p.a. real growth**.
- **Healthcare is ~38–40% of day-to-day government spending**.
- **Policy: 250 new Neighbourhood Health Centres** announced Autumn Budget 2025 (120 operational by 2030, part-funded via public-private partnership).
- **Reform: NHS England being abolished** and integrated into DHSC (saves ~£1bn/yr by end of parliament).

### 13. Spending on Education

- **UK education spending: ~4.1% of GDP in 2023/24** (down from 4.2% in 2022/23).
- **Total education spending: ~£116 billion 2024/25**.
- **Labour policy (2024):** VAT charged on private school fees (raises ~£1.5bn p.a.); revenue earmarked for 6,500 additional state-school teachers.

### 14. Spending on Infrastructure

- **UK Infrastructure: A 10-Year Strategy** (June 2025) — £725 billion of government funding committed over 10 years.
- **National Infrastructure Service and Transformation Authority (NISTA)** created in 2025 (merger of NIC and IPA).
- **Specific commitments:** £14.2bn Sizewell C nuclear, £9.4bn for carbon capture and storage, £13.2bn Warm Homes Plan, £4.2bn flood defences, £891m Lower Thames Crossing.
- **Issue:** UK has had the **lowest public investment in the G7 for 24 of the last 30 years**.

### 15. Government R&D Spending

- **Total UK R&D: ~2.9–3.0% of GDP** (after ONS methodology revisions) — UK is 4th highest in G7.
- **Government R&D budget: ~£20 billion p.a. by 2024/25**.
- **G7 comparison:** US 3.47%, Japan 3.27%, Germany 3.13%, UK ~2.9–3.0%.
- **Target:** 2.4% of GDP by 2027 — already exceeded post-revision.

### 16. Environmental Protection & Climate Policy

- **UK GHG emissions: 371 MtCO₂e in 2024** (territorial basis) — **a 54% reduction from 1990**.
- **Legal target:** **Net zero by 2050** (Climate Change Act 2008).
- **Interim targets:** 68% cut by 2030, **81% by 2035** vs 1990 (NDC announced at COP29 in November 2024).
- **Policies:**
  - **Carbon Budget and Growth Delivery Plan (October 2025)** — replaces earlier plan struck down by courts.
  - **Great British Energy Act** — publicly owned clean-power company.
  - **Last coal power station closed in 2024**.
  - **UK Emissions Trading Scheme** (post-Brexit replacement for EU ETS).
  - **Zero Emission Vehicle (ZEV) mandate** — phase-out of new petrol/diesel cars by 2030.
- **Cost estimate:** Climate Change Committee says net zero will cost ~0.2% of GDP p.a. on average to 2050.

### 17. Income Inequality

- **Gini coefficient (disposable income): 33% before housing costs, 37% after housing costs (2024/25)**.
- The Gini has been **broadly stable since the early 1990s** but is higher than in the 1960s–70s.
- **The richest 20% take ~36% of all income**; the poorest 20% take ~8%.
- **Median weekly income (BHC): £719 in 2024/25**.
- **The West Midlands** has the lowest regional median income (£646); **London** the highest (£789).

### 18. Wealth Inequality

- **Wealth Gini: 0.59 (2020–22)** — wealth is far more unequal than income.
- **Top 10% of households own ~43% of all wealth**; bottom 50% own just 9%.
- **Top decile threshold: £1,200,500+**; bottom decile: £16,500 or less.
- **Median household wealth: £293,700**.
- Wealth-and-Assets Survey lost its official-statistics accreditation in 2025 — treat figures with caution.

### 19. Financial Sector

- **Financial & insurance services: ~8% of UK GDP**.
- Contributes ~£100bn+ in tax revenue annually.
- London is one of the world's two leading global financial centres (alongside NYC).

### 20. Regulation in the Financial Sector

- **Three main regulators:**
  - **Bank of England (BoE)** — Financial Policy Committee (FPC) for macroprudential stability.
  - **Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA)** — within the BoE, supervises banks and insurers.
  - **Financial Conduct Authority (FCA)** — conduct, consumer protection, markets.
- **Key recent reforms (2025/26):**
  - **Leeds Reforms / Financial Services Growth & Competitiveness Strategy** (July 2025) — 10-year plan to "rewire" regulation for growth.
  - **FPC cut system-wide bank capital requirements from 14% to 13% (December 2025)** to support lending.
  - **Streamlining of Senior Managers & Certification Regime (SM&CR)** — April 2026.
  - **Basel 3.1** implementation delayed.
- **Origins:** Heavy regulation followed the 2007–08 Global Financial Crisis (Northern Rock, RBS, Lloyds bailouts).

### 21. Supply-Side Policies

- **Planning and Infrastructure Bill 2024–25** — reform to speed up Nationally Significant Infrastructure Projects.
- **Investment in skills** — apprenticeship reforms, Youth Guarantee.
- **R&D tax credits** worth ~£7 billion p.a.
- **Free Trade Zones (Freeports)** — 8 designated in England.
- **Industrial Strategy (June 2025)** — eight high-growth sectors (IS-8) including financial services, life sciences, AI.

### 22. National Minimum Wage / National Living Wage

- **National Living Wage (21+): £12.71 per hour from 1 April 2026** (up 4.1% from £12.21).
- **18–20 year olds: £10.85** (up 8.5% from £10.00).
- **16–17 / apprentices: £8.00** (up 6%).
- **Real Living Wage (voluntary): £13.45 UK / £14.80 London**.
- **~6% of jobs are paid at the NLW**, accounting for only 2.5% of the total wage bill.
- **Bank of England estimates** the NLW added only 0.06–0.24pp to inflation in April 2025.
- One of the **highest minimum wages in the OECD** as a share of median earnings.

### 23. Deregulation & Privatisation

- **Major privatisations (historical):** British Telecom (1984), British Gas (1986), water companies (1989), British Rail (1993–97), Royal Mail (2013).
- **Recent direction is mixed:**
  - **Re-nationalisations:** Most train operating companies returning to public ownership (e.g. South Western Railway, May 2025) under the Passenger Railway Services (Public Ownership) Act 2024.
  - **Water (Special Measures) Act 2025** — strengthens regulation rather than re-nationalisation.
- **Current deregulation:** FCA/PRA "growth and competitiveness" reforms, Leeds Reforms 2025.

### 24. Trade Unions

- **Union membership: 6.4 million (2024)** — 22.0% of UK employees, the lowest density on record.
- **Largest unions:** UNISON (~1.4m, public sector), Unite (~1.17m), GMB, NEU.
- **Public sector density: ~50%**; private sector: ~12%.
- **2022–24 strike wave:** rail (RMT/ASLEF), NHS, civil servants — driven by cost-of-living pressures.
- **Employment Rights Act 2025** (Labour): repealed Conservative anti-strike laws, removes 50% turnout requirement (from August 2026), restores stronger union rights.

### 25. Globalisation

- **Trade-to-GDP ratio: 62.5% in 2025**.
- The UK is a **net importer of goods**, **net exporter of services** — classic "post-industrial" pattern.
- Major beneficiary of foreign direct investment (FDI), especially in financial services.
- **Brexit (1 January 2021)** marked a significant reversal of integration with the UK's largest trading partner.

### 26. Largest Exports (2025)

- Total UK exports: **£930 billion** (goods + services).
- **Top destinations:** USA ($86.9bn, 15.6% of UK exports), Germany, Netherlands, Ireland, France, China.
- **Goods exports** include: machinery, pharmaceuticals (AstraZeneca, GSK), cars, aerospace (Rolls-Royce, Airbus wings), Scotch whisky, oil.
- **Services exports** are the UK's strength: financial services, professional/legal services, education, creative industries.
- **EU = 41% of UK exports in 2025**.

### 27. Largest Imports (2025)

- Total UK imports: **£969 billion** in 2025.
- Top sources: Germany, China, USA, Netherlands, France.
- **Main categories:** machinery, vehicles, oil and refined products, pharmaceuticals, food.
- **EU = 49% of UK imports in 2025**.

### 28. Tariffs

- Since Brexit, the UK has its own **UK Global Tariff (UKGT)**, applied to non-FTA imports.
- The UKGT removed or simplified many EU tariffs, freezing duties on £30bn of imports.
- **Tariff-free trade with the EU** (under the Trade and Cooperation Agreement, 2021) — but subject to **rules of origin** and non-tariff barriers.
- **CPTPP membership (15 December 2024)**: zero tariff on **99% of UK goods exports** to CPTPP members (e.g. Malaysian whisky tariff cut from 80% to 0%; Malaysian car tariff cut from 30% to 0%).
- **Trump 2.0 tariffs (2025):** USA imposed reciprocal tariffs disrupting global trade; UK negotiated some limited exemptions but is still affected.

### 29. Quotas

- **Tariff-rate quotas (TRQs)** apply to certain agricultural products (e.g. NZ lamb, Canadian/EU cheese).
- The UK inherited TRQs from EU membership and renegotiated them after Brexit.
- Under CPTPP, additional **TRQs were agreed** for sensitive products (e.g. butter, beef from Canada).

### 30. Trading Blocs & Monetary Unions

- **The UK is in no monetary union** — sterling is a free-floating currency. Voted by referendum to **stay out of the euro**.
- **EU–UK Trade and Cooperation Agreement (2021)** — UK is outside the Single Market and Customs Union.
- **CPTPP (December 2024)** — UK is the **first European and first acceding member**; brings 15% of world GDP.
- **Bilateral FTAs** with Australia, New Zealand, Japan, India (signed May 2025), and others.
- **EU is still the UK's biggest trading partner** (52% of goods trade).

## PART 2: SOUTH AFRICA (Developing/Emerging Economy)

### 1. Growth and Development

- **Real GDP growth: 1.1% in 2025** — the highest since 2022 (2.1%), but below long-run average (~3% historically) (StatsSA).
- **Q4 2025: +0.4% QoQ** — fifth consecutive quarter of growth, the longest unbroken expansion since 2018.
- **IMF forecast 2026: ~1.4% growth** (revised down from earlier given Iran/oil shock).
- **Long-run challenge:** GDP per capita is **below 2007 levels** (World Bank).
- **Structural constraints:** product- and labour-market rigidities, spatial inequality, weak infrastructure, governance issues.

### 2. HDI (Human Development Index)

- **South Africa HDI: 0.717 (2022, latest UNDP)** — "High Human Development".
- **Ranks 110th out of 193 countries**.
- Lower than HDI peers in Latin America despite similar income levels — reflects inequality and the legacy of apartheid.

### 3. Healthcare

- **Life expectancy: ~65 years** — held back by HIV/AIDS and TB.
- **Public/private split:** ~84% of the population uses **public healthcare** (under-resourced); ~16% private (well-funded medical aid schemes).
- **National Health Insurance (NHI) Act signed into law on 15 May 2024** — phased in 2024–2028. Aims for universal, single-payer healthcare to bridge the public/private divide.
- **Funded by:** general taxes, contributions by higher earners, employer contributions.
- **Healthcare spending: ~33% of provincial budgets**; ~9% of GDP in total (public + private combined).

### 4. Education

- **Education is the largest single line of provincial spending: ~41% of provincial budgets, ~R260 billion p.a.**
- **Education spending: ~6% of GDP** — high by international standards.
- **Outcomes lag spending:** South Africa typically scores **near the bottom of international assessments** (TIMSS, PIRLS) despite high spending. This is a major essay point on "quantity vs quality" of state provision.
- **Unemployment rates by education** (Q1 2025): no matric 51.6%; matric 47.6%; degree 23.9% — illustrating returns to education.

### 5. Inequality

- **Gini coefficient: ~0.63 (consumption); ~0.67 (income)** — **South Africa is the most unequal country in the World Bank's database of 164 countries.**
- **Richest 10% hold ~71% of the wealth; poorest 60% just 7%**.
- Inequality reflects spatial, racial and skill-based legacies of apartheid plus uneven access to land, education, and capital.
- **Social grants reach ~28 million people** — without them, inequality would be ~20 Gini points higher.

### 6. Poverty

- **Upper-bound poverty rate: 37.9% in 2023** (down from 46.7% in 2015) — improvement, but still very high (World Bank).
- **Nearly 60% live below the upper-middle-income poverty line.**
- About **64% of black South Africans** live below the upper poverty line (R1,634/month, October 2025).
- **Food poverty line ~R760/month**; **lower-bound R1,090/month** (StatsSA).

### 7. Corruption

- **Corruption Perceptions Index 2025: score 41/100, rank 81 out of 182** (Transparency International) — unchanged from 2023 and 2024.
- South Africa **lost ~10 points** between 2007 (51) and 2023 (41).
- **Zondo Commission (State Capture Inquiry, 2018–22)** exposed widespread looting of state-owned enterprises under former president Jacob Zuma.
- **Positive in 2025:** South Africa exited the **FATF grey list** in 2025; **S&P credit rating upgrade** for the first time in 16 years.

### 8. Biggest Exports (2025)

- **Total exports: ~$116.8 billion (2025)**, up 5.9% on 2024.
- **Top categories:** precious metals (gold, platinum group metals), iron ore, coal, cars and vehicles (BMW, Mercedes-Benz, Toyota all manufacture in SA), citrus and wine, manganese, chrome.
- **South Africa is the world's largest producer** of chrome, manganese, platinum, vanadium, and vermiculite.
- **Top destinations:** China ($12.8bn, 12.3% of exports), USA, Germany, Japan, UK, Botswana, Mozambique, Namibia, India.
- **Asia takes 33% of SA exports**, Africa 29%, Europe 27%, North America 9%.

### 9. Biggest Imports (2025)

- **Total imports: ~$105 billion** in 2025.
- **Top imports:** mineral fuels including oil ($18.5bn, 17.6%), machinery including computers ($14bn), electrical machinery ($10.4bn), vehicles, pharmaceuticals.
- **Top sources:** China (22.5%), India (7.3%), Germany (6.87%), USA (6.86%), Oman, Thailand, Saudi Arabia.
- **SA runs significant deficits with China and oil exporters** (Saudi Arabia, Oman).

### 10. Current Account

- **2025 current account deficit: R35.2 billion (0.5% of GDP)** — narrowed from R48 billion (0.7%) in 2024 (SARB).
- **Q4 2025: current account surplus of R50.2bn (0.6% of GDP)** — first surplus since Q3 2023, driven by a large trade surplus.
- **Goods trade surplus: R212bn (2.8% of GDP) in 2025**.
- **Services, income and current-transfer account deficit: 3.2% of GDP** — reflects investment income paid abroad and migrant remittances out.

### 11. Unemployment

- **Q4 2025: 31.4%** (official, narrow definition) — the lowest since Q3 2020.
- **Q1 2026: 32.7%** (rose again — labour force participation falling).
- **Expanded unemployment rate (incl. discouraged): 43.7% (Q1 2026)**.
- **~8.1 million unemployed**.
- **One of the highest unemployment rates in the world** — a structural problem rooted in apartheid-era spatial planning, skills mismatch, labour-market rigidity and slow growth.

### 12. Youth Unemployment

- **Ages 15–24: 60.9% in Q1 2026** (up from 57% in Q4 2025).
- **Ages 25–34: 40.6%**.
- **Ages 15–34 (broad youth): 46.1% in Q1 2025**.
- **NEET (Not in Employment, Education or Training) for 15–34s: 45.6%**.
- ~1.9 million young people are **discouraged work-seekers**.
- This is a major source of social and political risk — often called South Africa's "ticking time bomb".

### 13. Infrastructure

- **Electricity: Eskom** (state-owned monopoly generator).
  - **Energy Availability Factor (EAF): 64–70% in 2025/26** — recovering from <50% in 2023.
  - **300+ consecutive days without load shedding** as of March 2026; only 26 hours of load shedding in all of FY 2025/26 (April–May 2025).
  - **Generation Recovery Plan** delivered turnaround.
  - **~18 GW of private generation** now in the pipeline (~25% of installed capacity) following reforms.
- **Logistics: Transnet** (rail, ports). Freight rail volumes fell from 226 Mt (2017/18) to 151 Mt (2023/24). Reforms in 2025 opened rail and ports to private competition.
- **Water:** ongoing reforms to municipal supply; many municipalities failing.
- **State guarantees to SOEs: R513bn → R661bn (2025→2026)** — Eskom is 73.6% of this exposure.

### 14. Policies Used to Increase Growth and Development

- **Operation Vulindlela (Phase I from 2020; Phase II from April 2025)** — joint Presidency/Treasury initiative to drive structural reform in:
  - Electricity (private generation, restructure of Eskom into separate generation/transmission/distribution entities)
  - Freight rail and ports (private access to Transnet rail; Rail Infrastructure Manager established 2025)
  - Water and municipal services
  - Digital/telecommunications (lower data costs, faster spectrum release)
  - Visa reform (Electronic Travel Authorisation system live September 2025)
- **National Development Plan 2030** — overarching strategic framework.
- **Black Economic Empowerment (BEE) / B-BBEE** — targets for ownership, management and procurement.
- **Industrial Policy Action Plan (IPAP)** — supports targeted manufacturing.
- **2025 lower inflation target (3% instead of 3–6% midpoint)** — strengthens macro credibility.

### 15. Inflation

- **Headline CPI: 3.1% (March 2026)**, down from 3.6% in Dec 2025.
- **2025 annual average: 3.2%** — the **lowest in 21 years** (StatsSA/SARB).
- **2026 forecast: ~3.7%** (rising temporarily due to Middle East oil shock; SARB expects fuel inflation >18% in Q2 2026).
- **New inflation target: 3% ± 1pp** (adopted 12 November 2025, replacing the 3–6% range in place since 2000). National Treasury sets the target; SARB pursues it.
- **SARB Policy Rate (repo rate): 6.75% (held since March 2026)**, after a 25bp cut to 6.75% in November 2025. Prime lending rate: 10.25%.

### 16. Government Debt

- **Gross loan debt: 78.8% of GDP at end-2025** (SARB/CEIC).
- Up from 23.5% in 2008 — a major fiscal deterioration over 17 years.
- **Treasury projects debt to peak in 2025/26** and decline thereafter.
- **Debt service costs: ~21 cents of every R1 of government spending** — crowding out essential services.
- **2025: first sovereign credit-rating upgrade in 16 years** from S&P.

### 17. Budget Deficit

- **Main budget deficit: ~4.5% of GDP in 2025** (Treasury).
- **Primary surplus: 0.9% of GDP in 2025/26**, projected to rise to **2.3% of GDP by 2028/29** under the Medium-Term Budget Policy Statement (MTBPS Nov 2025).
- **First primary surplus since 2008/09** — a key fiscal-consolidation milestone.
- Achieved through wage-bill control, SOE oversight, slower spending growth.

## TIPS FOR APPLICATION IN ESSAYS

1. **Always date your figures** — "UK CPI was 3.3% in March 2026" beats "UK inflation is high".
2. **Pair a number with a cause** — link the data to a theory (e.g. UK Q1 2026 GDP growth +0.6% driven by services and household consumption — link to MPC, real wages turning positive).
3. **Use comparison** — UK 4.9% unemployment vs SA 32.7% drives home a powerful contrast about developed vs developing economies.
4. **Use policy examples to evaluate** — NHI in SA, NLW in UK, Operation Vulindlela, CPTPP — each gives a sentence of evaluation worth a mark or two.
5. **Quote sources** — "ONS", "SARB", "World Bank", "Transparency International" all add credibility.
