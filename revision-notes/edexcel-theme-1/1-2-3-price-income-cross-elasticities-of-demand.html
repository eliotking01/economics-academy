1.2.3 Price, Income & Cross Elasticities of Demand

Edexcel A-Level Economics (9EC0) | Theme 1.2.3

Specification Coverage: This topic requires students to understand, calculate, and interpret PED, YED, and XED values. Students should be able to analyse how these elasticities influence firm revenue and government policy decisions, using real-world examples.

Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

Definition: Measures the responsiveness of quantity demanded (QD) to a change in price (P), calculated as:

PED = %ΔQD ÷ %ΔP
Calculation Protocol: Always use the midpoint method for accuracy: %Δ = (New - Old) ÷ [(New + Old)/2] × 100. This avoids base-value bias when prices rise/fall from different starting points.

Worked Example

Scenario: Price ↑ £10 → £15 | QD ↓ 100 → 40 units
Step 1: %ΔQD (40-100) ÷ [(40+100)/2] × 100 = -85.7%
Step 2: %ΔP (15-10) ÷ [(15+10)/2] × 100 = +40%
Step 3: PED -85.7 ÷ 40 = -2.14 (Elastic)
Real-World Application: Netflix's 2022 price hike (Standard plan £9.99 → £10.99) resulted in only 3% subscriber loss (PED = -0.27), demonstrating inelastic demand for streaming services among loyal users.

PED Classification Spectrum

Value Range Elasticity Type Characteristics Examples
PED = 0 Perfectly Inelastic QD unchanged by price Insulin (life-saving drugs)
0 < |PED| < 1 Relatively Inelastic %ΔQD < %ΔP Petrol (0.4), Cigarettes (0.5)
|PED| = 1 Unitary Elastic %ΔQD = %ΔP Theoretical benchmark
1 < |PED| < ∞ Relatively Elastic %ΔQD > %ΔP Restaurant meals (2.3), Foreign holidays (3.8)
PED = ∞ Perfectly Elastic Infinite QD response Agricultural commodities at fixed prices

Critical Determinants of PED

  1. Substitutability: More substitutes → Higher |PED| (e.g., Coca-Cola vs generic cola)
  2. Necessity vs Luxury: Essentials tend to be inelastic (bread: 0.2) vs luxuries elastic (designer handbags: 4.1)
  3. Time Lag: Short-run PED for petrol: 0.2 | Long-run: 0.7 (find alternatives)
  4. Proportion of Income: Big-ticket items (cars: 1.2) vs small purchases (salt: 0.1)

Income Elasticity of Demand (YED)

Definition: Measures how QD responds to changes in consumer income (Y):

YED = %ΔQD ÷ %ΔY

YED Typology

YED Range Good Classification Economic Implications
YED < 0 Inferior Goods Demand ↓ as income ↑ (Bus travel: -0.4)
0 < YED < 1 Normal Necessities Demand ↑ proportionally less than income ↑ (Food: 0.3)
YED > 1 Normal Luxuries Demand ↑ proportionally more than income ↑ (Sports cars: 2.5)
Business Strategy Insight: During the 2020 pandemic, Lidl reported 12% sales growth as YED data showed consumers switching from Waitrose (YED: 1.2) to discounters (YED: -0.3) during income shocks.

Cross Elasticity of Demand (XED)

Definition: Measures responsiveness of QD for Good A to price changes in Good B:

XED = %ΔQDA ÷ %ΔPB

XED Interpretation Guide

XED Value Relationship Real-World Example
XED < 0 Complements PlayStation consoles (-1.8) & games
XED = 0 Unrelated Butter (0.02) & mobile phones
XED > 0 Substitutes Coca-Cola (+0.8) & Pepsi
Evaluation Tip: The magnitude matters as much as the sign. XED of -0.2 suggests weak complements, while -2.5 indicates strong complements. Always contextualize the value.

Strategic Applications

Firm-Level Decisions

Pricing Strategy
  • Inelastic goods: Price ↑ → Revenue ↑ (Pharmaceuticals)
  • Elastic goods: Price ↓ → Revenue ↑ (Airline seats)
Product Portfolio
  • Balance inferior (YED < 0) & normal goods (YED > 0) for economic cycles
  • Bundle strong complements (XED < -1) to increase sales

Government Policy

Objective Tool Elasticity Consideration
Reduce smoking Excise tax PED = -0.4 → 10% price ↑ → 4% QD ↓
Encourage renewables Subsidies Solar panels: PED = -1.8 → 10% price ↓ → 18% QD ↑
