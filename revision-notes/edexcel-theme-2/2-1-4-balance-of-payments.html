2.1.4 Balance of Payments

Edexcel A-Level Economics (9EC0) | Theme 2.1.4

Specification Coverage: Students must understand the structure of the balance of payments (current account + financial account), analyse causes and consequences of current account deficits, and evaluate policies to correct imbalances. Analysis should include recent UK data and global trade relationships.

Structure of the Balance of Payments

Fundamental Identity: Current Account + Financial Account + Capital Account = 0

Component Definition UK 2023 (£bn)
Current Account Goods, services, income & transfers -£78.2 (-3.1% GDP)
Financial Account Investment flows + reserve changes +£82.4
Capital Account Non-produced assets + debt forgiveness -£4.2
Key Concept: A current account deficit must be matched by a financial account surplus - the UK attracts foreign investment to fund its trade gap.

Causes of UK Current Account Deficit

Structural Factors

  • Deindustrialisation (manufacturing 9% GDP vs 15% in 2000)
  • North Sea oil decline (net exporter → net importer)
  • High marginal propensity to import (MPM = 0.32)

Competitiveness Issues

  • Productivity growth only 0.4% pa (vs Germany 1.2%)
  • Non-price factors (design, quality lag)
  • Brexit trade friction (goods exports to EU ↓15%)

Exchange Rate Effects

  • Sterling depreciation (2016-2023) failed to correct deficit
  • J-curve effect (short-run ↑import costs)
  • Inelastic demand for key imports (energy, medicines)
2023 Energy Shock: Russia-Ukraine war widened goods deficit by £42bn (energy imports ↑58%), demonstrating vulnerability to external shocks.

Consequences of Deficits

Stakeholder Short-Term Impact Long-Term Risk
Consumers Wider choice of imports Living standards vulnerable to sterling crises
Firms Cheaper imported inputs Foreign ownership ↑ (72% FTSE 100)
Government Tax revenue from consumption Policy constraints (austerity pressures)
Economy AD boost from net imports Financing risk if investors lose confidence
Evaluation Point: The UK's deficit is sustainable while:
  • Financial account remains in surplus
  • Sterling maintains reserve currency status
  • Services surplus offsets goods deficit

Policy Solutions

Policy Mechanism UK Example Limitation
Depreciation BoE lowers interest rates 2016 Brexit vote: £ fell 15% J-curve effect (2-3 year lag)
Protectionism Tariffs/quotas on imports EU steel tariffs (2022) Retaliation risk (WTO rules)
Supply-Side Improve competitiveness £3.8bn skills fund 5-10 year time lag
Demand-Side Reduce consumption 2023 interest rates ↑ to 5.25% Recession risk
