2.1.2 Inflation

Edexcel A-Level Economics (9EC0) | Theme 2.1.2

Specification Coverage: Students must understand how inflation is measured (CPI/RPI), distinguish between demand-pull and cost-push inflation, and evaluate the impact of inflation on different economic agents. Analysis should include current UK inflation data and policy responses.

Key Definitions

Term Definition UK Example (2024)
Inflation Sustained rise in general price level (CPI) 3.2% (Feb 2024, ONS)
Deflation Negative inflation (falling prices) Last occurred in 2015 (-0.1%)
Disinflation Slowing inflation rate (prices still rising) Peaked at 11.1% in 2022 → 3.2%
Target Alert: Bank of England's CPI target is 2% (±1%). Current rate (3.2%) indicates mildly inflationary gap requiring potential monetary policy response.

Measuring Inflation: CPI Methodology

  1. Basket Selection: 700 goods/services representing average UK household spending (updated annually)
  2. Weighting: Housing (34%), Transport (11%), Food (9%) etc. (2024 weights)
  3. Price Collection: 150,000 prices gathered monthly from 20,000 outlets
  4. Calculation:
    \[ \text{CPI} = \frac{\text{Cost of basket in current year}}{\text{Cost of basket in base year}} \times 100 \]

CPI Calculation Example

Base Year (2020): Basket cost = £400
2023 Basket Cost: £544.05
CPI Calculation: \(\frac{544.05}{400} \times 100 = 136.01\)
Inflation Rate: \(\frac{136.01 - 108.75}{108.75} \times 100 = 25.07\%\)

CPI vs RPI

Feature CPI RPI
Coverage Excludes housing costs Includes mortgage interest, council tax
Formula Geometric mean Arithmetic mean
Current Rate 3.2% 4.5% (Feb 2024)
Use BoE target, pensions Train tickets, student loans

CPI Limitations

  1. Substitution bias: Fixed weights don't account for consumer switching
  2. Regional variation: London inflation typically 1.5× national average
  3. Quality changes: Doesn't fully adjust for improved tech (e.g., smartphones)
  4. New products: 12-month lag adding items (e.g., vaping products)

Causes of Inflation

1. Demand-Pull Inflation

Mechanism: Excess AD → Output gap → Price rises

  • UK 2021: Post-lockdown spending + savings = 5.4% inflation
  • Key drivers: Consumption (65% AD), Government spending (20%)
Diagram Alert 1: AD/AS diagram showing:
  • Rightward AD shift
  • Price level increase
  • Output gap at LRAS

2. Cost-Push Inflation

Mechanism: Rising costs → SRAS leftward shift

  • UK 2022: Energy prices ↑ 54% → 11.1% inflation
  • Key drivers: Import prices (20% goods), Wage growth (6.6% in 2023)
Diagram Alert 2: AD/AS diagram showing:
  • Leftward SRAS shift
  • Price level increase
  • Lower real output
Wage-Price Spiral: 2023 saw average wages rise 6.6% while productivity grew only 0.4%, creating persistent inflationary pressures (ONS).

Consequences of Inflation

Stakeholder Impact 2024 Example
Consumers Real income falls if wages don't keep pace 2023: Real wages fell 2.3% (TUC)
Firms Uncertainty reduces investment (↓18% in 2023) "Menu costs" ≈ 1.5% revenue (BoE)
Government Debt servicing costs rise (10yr gilt yield = 4.1%) Benefits indexation cost £4bn extra
Workers Industrial action if real wages fall 2023: 3.8m strike days (highest since 1990)
Evaluation Tip: Moderate inflation (2-3%) can be beneficial - encourages spending and investment. Only high/unexpected inflation causes significant welfare loss.
