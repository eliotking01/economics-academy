Theme 3.3.3: Economies & Diseconomies of Scale

1\. The Long-Run Average Cost (LRAC) Curve

In the **long run**, firms can change their scale of production. This
leads to **economies** and **diseconomies** of scale, shaping the LRAC
curve.

(Hand-draw Diagram: The LRAC Curve & MES)

Draw axes: Cost per unit (£) vs Output (Scale).

Draw a U-shaped LRAC curve.

Label the downward-sloping section "Economies of Scale".

Label the upward-sloping section "Diseconomies of Scale".

Mark the **lowest point** of the curve. Label this the **Minimum
Efficient Scale (MES)**.

2\. Economies of Scale (Decreasing LRAC)

Cost advantages a firm gains by **increasing its scale of
output**. **Internal** to the firm.

|  |  |  |
|----|----|----|
| Type | Explanation | Example |
| Purchasing | Bulk-buying discounts on raw materials. | A large supermarket chain gets lower prices from suppliers. |
| Technical | Use of more efficient, large-scale machinery/tech. | A car factory uses robotic assembly lines. |
| Financial | Easier & cheaper access to finance (lower interest rates). | A large PLC gets a better loan rate than a small business. |
| Managerial | Specialisation of management (e.g., separate HR, Finance depts). |  |
| Marketing | Spreading advertising costs over a larger output. | A global brand's TV ad cost per unit sold is tiny. |
| Risk-bearing | Diversification across products/markets spreads risk. | A conglomerate can absorb losses in one area. |

3\. Diseconomies of Scale (Increasing LRAC)

Cost disadvantages a firm faces when it **becomes too
large**. **Internal** to the firm.

|  |  |
|----|----|
| Type | Explanation |
| Management | **Bureaucracy, slow decision-making**, loss of control as hierarchy grows. |
| Communication | Miscommunication, distortion of messages across many layers/departments. |
| Motivational | Workers feel alienated, leading to lower productivity (principal-agent problem). |
| Geographical | Coordination problems between distant plants/offices. |

4\. Minimum Efficient Scale (MES)

The **lowest point** on the LRAC curve.

The **output level** where the firm achieves the **lowest possible cost
per unit**.

Firms operating at or near MES have a **significant cost advantage**.

5\. External Economies of Scale

Cost advantages that benefit **all firms in a growing
industry** (external to the individual firm).

|  |  |  |
|----|----|----|
| Source | Explanation | Example |
| Geographic Clusters | Ancillary firms locate nearby, reducing transport/logistics costs. | Tech firms in Silicon Valley. |
| Skilled Labour Pools | Concentration of industry attracts specialised workers, reducing training costs. | Financial services in London. |
| Improved Infrastructure | Transport/communications improve to serve the industry. | Road/rail links near a port. |
| Knowledge Spillovers | Sharing of ideas/innovation between firms in close proximity. |  |

6\. Exam Focus: Key Takeaways

- **Define** economies and diseconomies of scale.

- **Draw and interpret** the **LRAC curve**, labelling the sections for
  economies/diseconomies and the **MES**.

- Identify, define, and give examples of different internal economies of
  scale.

- Explain the causes of diseconomies of scale.

- **Understand** the concept of **external economies of scale** and how
  they differ from internal ones.
