Theme 1.3.3: Public Goods

1\. Characteristics of Public Goods

Public goods possess two defining characteristics that prevent their
provision by the free market:

**Non-Excludability:** It is **impossible or extremely costly to prevent
non-payers** from consuming the good once it is provided (e.g., you
cannot stop someone from benefiting from street lighting or national
defence).

**Non-Rivalry:** One person's consumption **does not reduce the amount
available** for others. The marginal cost of supplying an additional
user is zero (e.g., an extra person watching a firework display does not
diminish others' view).

2\. The Free Market Problem & The Free Rider Issue

Because public goods are **non-excludable**, firms cannot use the price
mechanism to charge users. This leads to the **free rider problem**:
individuals have no incentive to pay for the good, as they can benefit
from it without contributing, knowing others will pay.

**Consequence:** Since everyone acts as a free rider, **no one pays**.
Private firms therefore have **no profit incentive** to supply public
goods, leading to their complete **under-provision or non-provision**—a
clear market failure.

3\. Government Provision

Due to this market failure, public goods are typically **provided by the
government** and financed through taxation. This ensures society
benefits from goods that are socially desirable but unprofitable to
produce privately.

**Examples:** National defence, street lighting, lighthouses, public
parks, and basic scientific research.

Key Exam Tips:

**Test for a Public Good:** For a **pure public good**, it must
satisfy **both** conditions: **non-excludable AND non-rival**. If it
fails one, it is a **quasi-public good** (e.g., a toll road is
excludable but non-rival up to congestion; it can be provided
privately).

**Link to Market Failure:** Public goods are a **core type of market
failure**. The free market fails because it cannot overcome the free
rider problem.

Key Terminology: Always use and define the terms non-excludability,
non-rivalry, and free rider problem in your answers.
