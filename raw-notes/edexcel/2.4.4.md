Theme 2.4.4: The Multiplier

1\. What is the Multiplier?

**Definition:** The process by which an **initial injection** (e.g., an
increase in G, I, or X) into the circular flow leads to a **larger final
increase** in national income (Real GDP).

Multiplier Ratio: Multiplier (k) = (Final Change in Real GDP) / (Initial
Injection)

E.g., if a £2bn increase in government spending causes a £6bn rise in
GDP, the multiplier is **3**.

2\. The Multiplier Process: How it Works

**Initial Injection:** An increase in spending (e.g., government builds
a new hospital).

**Income:** This spending becomes income for builders, architects, etc.

Spending: These recipients spend a proportion of their new income
(their Marginal Propensity to Consume - MPC).

**Further Rounds:** This spending becomes income for others (e.g.,
shopkeepers), who also spend a proportion. This creates successive
rounds of spending.

**Total Effect:** The sum of all these rounds of spending is
a **multiple** of the initial injection.

3\. The Role of Marginal Propensities

These determine **what happens to each extra £ of income** and are
crucial for the multiplier size.

|  |  |  |
|----|----|----|
| Term | What it means | Formula |
| **MPC** (Marginal Propensity to Consume) | Proportion of extra income **spent** on domestic goods/services. | ΔC / ΔY |
| **MPS** (Marginal Propensity to Save) | Proportion of extra income **saved**. | ΔS / ΔY |
| **MPT** (Marginal Propensity to Tax) | Proportion of extra income **paid in tax**. | ΔT / ΔY |
| **MPM** (Marginal Propensity to Import) | Proportion of extra income **spent on imports**. | ΔM / ΔY |

**Key Relationship:** **MPC + MPS + MPT + MPM = 1** (All extra income is
consumed, saved, taxed, or spent on imports).

4\. Calculating the Multiplier

The size of the multiplier **(k)** depends
on **leakages** (withdrawals). Higher leakages mean a smaller
multiplier.

- Formula 1 (using MPC): k = 1 / (1 - MPC)

- Formula 2 (using withdrawals): k = 1 / (MPS + MPT + MPM) = 1 / MPW\
  (Where MPW = Marginal Propensity to Withdraw)

**Example:** If MPC = 0.8, then MPS+MPT+MPM = 0.2.

- k = 1 / (1 - 0.8) = 1 / 0.2 = \*\*5\*\*

- An initial £1bn injection would lead to a **£5bn** final increase in
  GDP.

6\. Factors Affecting the Size of the Multiplier

- **High MPC/Low Leakages** → **Larger Multiplier.** (e.g., in a
  recession with low confidence, the MPC may be low, reducing the
  multiplier).

- **High Tax Rates (MPT)** → Smaller Multiplier (more leakage).

- **High Propensity to Import (MPM)** → Smaller Multiplier (spending
  leaks abroad).

- **High Interest Rates** → Encourages saving (MPS↑) → Smaller
  Multiplier.

- **The multiplier also works in reverse** (Negative Multiplier Effect):
  A reduction in injections causes a multiplied contraction in national
  income.

7\. Significance for Government Policy

- **Fiscal Policy:** Knowing the approximate multiplier helps
  governments estimate the impact of changes in **G** or **T** on
  economic growth.

- **Time Lags:** The multiplier process takes time to work through the
  economy.

- **Spare Capacity:** The multiplier effect on **output** is larger if
  the economy has **spare capacity** (Keynesian horizontal AS). If at
  full capacity, the multiplier mainly increases **prices**.

8\. Exam Focus: Key Takeaways

- **Define** the multiplier and **explain the process** using the
  circular flow.

- Calculate the multiplier using the formulas k = 1/(1-MPC) or k =
  1/(MPS+MPT+MPM).

- **Draw** the multiplier effect on an AD/AS diagram.

- **Analyse** how changes in economic factors (e.g., tax rates, exchange
  rates, confidence) affect the size of the multiplier.

- **Evaluate** the importance of the multiplier for government fiscal
  policy.
