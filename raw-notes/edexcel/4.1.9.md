Theme 4.1.9: International Competitiveness

1\. Definition and Measures

International competitiveness is the ability of a country's goods and
services to **compete successfully in global markets**. Key measures
include:

**Relative Unit Labour Costs:** (Total Labour Costs / Total Output).
Lower relative costs indicate higher competitiveness.

**Relative Export Prices:** Rising export prices relative to competitors
indicates falling competitiveness.

**Global Market Share:** A rising share of world exports indicates
strong competitiveness.

2\. Factors Influencing Competitiveness

Competitiveness is **relative** and depends on **cost and
non-cost** factors:

Cost Factors (Affecting Price):

Relative Productivity: Higher labour productivity lowers unit costs.

**Relative Inflation:** Higher inflation than rivals erodes price
competitiveness.

**Relative Labour Costs:** Wages and non-wage costs (e.g., employer
pensions) relative to other countries.

**Exchange Rate:** A weaker currency lowers foreign prices of exports
(improving price competitiveness).

Non-Cost/Quality Factors (Affecting Demand):

Product Quality, Design & Innovation

Reliability and After-Sales Service

Strength of Branding and Marketing

**Infrastructure** (e.g., transport, digital networks) that supports
efficient trade.

3\. Significance of Competitiveness

Benefits of High Competitiveness:

**Export-Led Growth:** Boosts Aggregate Demand (AD) and GDP.

**Improved Current Account:** Moves the balance towards a surplus.

**Increased FDI:** Attracts inward investment.

Higher Employment & Living Standards from economic growth.

Problems of Low Competitiveness:

**Current Account Deficits,** leading to rising foreign debt.

Loss of Industries & Jobs (deindustrialisation).

**Lower Economic Growth** and potential recessions.

**Policy Pressure:** Forces governments into difficult trade-offs when
trying to restore competitiveness (e.g., austerity, devaluation).

Key Exam Tips:

**Relative Concept:** Always frame your analysis in **comparative
terms** (e.g., "UK productivity growth *relative to* Germany").

**Two-Sided Analysis:** Competitiveness isn't just about low costs
(**price competitiveness**); **non-price competitiveness** (quality,
innovation) is increasingly important for developed economies like the
UK.

**Link to Policies:** Improving competitiveness is a key aim
of **supply-side policies** (e.g., education, infrastructure, R&D
subsidies). However, these policies have high opportunity costs and long
time lags.

**Evaluation:** There can be a **trade-off** between competitiveness and
other objectives. For example, a weak currency boosts competitiveness
but can cause **imported inflation**. High competitiveness driven by low
wages may reduce living standards.
