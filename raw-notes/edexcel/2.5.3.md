Theme 2.5.3: The Trade (Business) Cycle

1\. What is the Trade Cycle?

**Definition:** The **fluctuations in real GDP (actual growth)** over
time, characterised by periods of boom and recession.

**Key Idea:** Actual GDP rises and falls around the
underlying **long-term trend rate of growth** (the path of potential
GDP, or LRAS).

2\. The Stages of the Trade Cycle

(Hand-draw Diagram: The Trade Cycle)

Draw a wavy line that fluctuates **around** this trend line.

Label the four key phases in sequence:

**Boom / Peak** (top of the cycle, above trend)

**Slowdown / Downturn / Recession** (downward slope, moving below trend)

**Trough / Recession** (bottom of the cycle, furthest below
trend) \[Note: A technical recession is two consecutive quarters of
negative growth\].

**Recovery / Expansion** (upward slope, moving back towards trend).

3\. Characteristics of Each Phase

|  |  |
|----|----|
| Boom / Peak (Positive Output Gap) | Recession / Trough (Negative Output Gap) |
| High/rising economic growth (actual GDP \> trend). | Low/negative economic growth (actual GDP \< trend). |
| Demand-pull inflation (rising prices). | Low inflation or deflationary pressure. |
| **Low unemployment** (labour shortages). | High & rising unemployment (cyclical unemployment). |
| High consumer & business confidence ('animal spirits'). | **Low confidence**; delayed investment and spending. |
| **Government budget improves** (higher tax revenues, lower welfare spending). | **Government budget deteriorates** (lower tax revenues, higher welfare spending). |
| **Possible current account deficit** (high domestic demand sucks in imports). | **Current account may improve** (lower domestic demand reduces imports). |

4\. Output Gaps and the Trade Cycle

- During a **boom**, the economy operates with a **positive output
  gap** (actual output \> potential output).

- During a **recession**, the economy operates with a **negative output
  gap** (actual output \< potential output).

- The trend line represents the path of **potential output** (LRAS).
  Fluctuations around it represent changes in **Aggregate Demand (AD)**.

5\. Causes of the Cycle

**Changes in Aggregate Demand (AD):** The primary driver. Fluctuations
in **investment (I)** (the most volatile component) and **consumer
confidence** are key.

**External Shocks:** Supply-side shocks (e.g., oil price spikes) or
demand-side shocks (e.g., a global financial crisis) can trigger or
deepen cycles.

**Inventory Cycles:** Firms building up and then depleting stocks of
goods.

**Credit Cycles:** Periods of easy borrowing (booms) followed by credit
crunches (recessions).

6\. The Role of Government (Demand-Side Stabilisation Policy)

Governments and central banks aim to **smooth the cycle** (reduce the
severity of booms and recessions).

**During a Boom:** Use **contractionary** policies (↑ interest rates, ↑
taxes, ↓ govt spending) to cool AD and control inflation.

**During a Recession:** Use **expansionary** policies (↓ interest rates,
↓ taxes, ↑ govt spending) to boost AD, close the negative output gap,
and reduce unemployment.
