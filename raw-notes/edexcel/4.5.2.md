Theme 4.5.2: Taxation

1\. Types of Tax Systems

**Progressive Tax:** The **% of income paid in tax rises** as income
rises (e.g., UK Income Tax). **Redistributes income**, improves equity.

**Proportional (Flat) Tax:** A **constant % of income** is paid in tax,
regardless of income level.

Regressive Tax: The % of income paid in tax falls as income rises
(e.g., VAT, excise duties). Places a higher relative burden on the poor.

2\. Direct vs. Indirect Taxes

Direct Taxes: Levied on income or profits (e.g., Income Tax, Corporation
Tax, National Insurance). Tend to be progressive.

Indirect Taxes: Levied on spending (e.g., VAT, Excise Duties). Tend to
be regressive.

3\. The Laffer Curve & Tax Revenues

Illustrates the theoretical relationship between **tax
rates** and **total tax revenue**.

(Hand-draw Diagram: The Laffer Curve)

Draw axes: Y-axis = Total Tax Revenue, X-axis = Tax Rate (%).

Draw a curve starting at the origin (0% rate = £0 revenue), rising to
a **peak**, then falling back to zero at 100%.

Label the peak point **A** (the optimal tax rate for maximising
revenue).

On the **left of the peak**, raising tax rates **increases revenue**.

On the **right of the peak**, raising tax rates **decreases
revenue** (due to severe disincentives to work/invest and increased tax
avoidance/evasion).

4\. Economic Effects of Changes in Tax Rates

|  |  |  |
|----|----|----|
| Economic Variable | Effect of an **INCREASE** in Taxes | Effect of a **DECREASE** in Taxes |
| Incentive to Work/Invest | ↓ **Incentive** (lower net reward). Can discourage entrepreneurship, overtime, and FDI. | ↑ **Incentive** (higher net reward). Encourages work, investment, and risk-taking. |
| Aggregate Demand (AD) | ↓ **AD** (↑ withdrawals from circular flow). Contractionary fiscal policy. | ↑ **AD** (↓ withdrawals). Expansionary fiscal policy. |
| Real Output & Employment | Likely to ↓ due to falling AD. | Likely to ↑ due to rising AD. |
| Income Distribution | ↑ **Equality** if progressive taxes are raised. | ↓ **Equality** if progressive taxes are cut. |
| Price Level (Inflation) | **Indirect tax** ↑ **= Cost-push inflation**. Direct tax ↑ may reduce demand-pull pressure. | **Indirect tax** ↓ **reduces cost-push**. Direct tax ↓ may increase demand-pull pressure. |
| Trade Balance | May **improve** as lower AD reduces import spending. | May **worsen** as higher AD increases import spending. |
| Government Budget | ↑ **Revenue** (if on left side of Laffer Curve). | ↓ **Revenue** (unless on right side of Laffer Curve). |

5\. Tax Incidence & Elasticity

The actual burden (incidence) of a tax depends on the Price Elasticity
of Demand (PED) and Supply (PES).

**Indirect Tax Example:** If demand is **inelastic**, **consumers bear
most of the tax** (price rises significantly). If demand
is **elastic**, **producers bear most of the tax**.
