Theme 2.1.1: Economic Growth

1\. Measuring Economic Growth: Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

Definition: GDP is the total monetary value of all final goods and
services produced within a country's borders in a given year. It is the
primary measure of economic activity and growth.

Calculation Methods:

**Expenditure Approach:** GDP = C + I + G + (X - M)\
(Consumption + Investment + Government Spending + Net Exports)

**Income Approach:** Sum of all incomes earned (wages, rent, interest,
profit). Both methods should equal the same total.

2\. Key GDP Adjustments for Accurate Comparisons

- **Nominal GDP:** The raw value, **not adjusted for inflation**. It
  reflects current prices.

- **Real GDP:** Nominal GDP **adjusted for inflation** using a price
  index (e.g., GDP deflator). This shows the true change in
  the **volume** of output and is the key measure of **economic
  growth**.

- **GDP per Capita:** Real GDP divided by the population. A better
  indicator of **average living standards** than total GDP.

- **Gross National Income (GNI):** GDP **plus** net income from abroad
  (e.g., profits sent home by citizens overseas, minus income sent out
  of the country by foreign nationals). GNI per capita can be a more
  accurate measure of a nation's income.

3\. Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)

- A conversion factor that accounts for **differences in the cost of
  living** between countries.

- It adjusts income figures to show how much a standard basket of goods
  can be bought locally, allowing for more meaningful comparisons
  of **real living standards**.

4\. Limitations of Using GDP to Compare Living Standards

GDP data, even when adjusted, has significant shortcomings for welfare
comparisons:

- **Doesn't Measure Inequality:** A high GDP per capita can mask severe
  income/wealth disparities.

- **Ignores Non-Monetary Factors:** It excludes the value of **unpaid
  work** (e.g., volunteering, childcare), leisure time, and
  environmental quality.

- **Doesn't Account for 'Bads':** Increased GDP may come from activities
  that reduce welfare (e.g., pollution, congestion) or from repairing
  damage (the 'broken window fallacy').

- **Quality Changes:** It struggles to account for improvements in
  product quality and variety.

- **Informal/Black Economy:** Large unrecorded economic activity
  distorts the figures.

Key Exam Tips:

- **Growth vs. Level:** Distinguish between the **rate of economic
  growth** (% change in real GDP) and the **level of GDP**.

- **Must-Know Adjustments:** Always use **real GDP per capita** for
  comparing living standards over time. For international
  comparisons, **real GNI per capita at PPP** is superior.

- **Evaluation:** This is a major evaluation topic. You must be able to
  argue why a rise in GDP may **not** equate to a rise in economic
  welfare, citing specific limitations. The **Easterlin
  Paradox** (happiness rises with income only up to a point) is a useful
  concept.

- **Alternative Measures:** Be aware of broader measures like
  the **Human Development Index (HDI)** or national happiness indices,
  which include health and education data.
