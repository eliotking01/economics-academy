Theme 2.2.3: Investment (I)

1\. Definition and Distinction

**Investment** is spending by firms on **capital goods** (e.g.,
machinery, factories, technology) to increase future productive
capacity.

**Gross Investment:** Total spending on new capital and replacement of
worn-out/depreciated capital.

**Net Investment:** Gross investment minus **depreciation** (capital
consumption). This measures the net **addition to the capital
stock** and is the key to increasing an economy's **productive
potential**.

2\. Key Influences on Investment

Investment is a volatile component of AD, heavily influenced
by **business confidence** and **expected rates of return**.

A. The Rate of Economic Growth (Demand Side)

- High and rising growth (**accelerator effect**) signals strong future
  demand, encouraging firms to invest in new capacity to meet it.

- Low or negative growth discourages investment due to weak demand and
  excess existing capacity.

B. Interest Rates & Cost of Capital (Cost Side)

- Higher interest rates:

  - Increase the cost of borrowing to finance investment.

  - Increase the opportunity cost of using retained profits (they could
    earn interest if saved).

  - Reduce the **Net Present Value (NPV)** of future investment
    projects, making them less profitable.

- **Lower interest rates** have the opposite effect, stimulating
  investment.

C. Business Confidence & 'Animal Spirits'

This is a crucial subjective factor. Confidence in future profits,
political stability, and the economic outlook drives investment.

**Keynes' 'Animal Spirits'** refers to the herd-like optimism or
pessimism of investors, which can cause investment to swing wildly,
independent of interest rates.

D. Government Policy & Regulation

**Incentives:** **Subsidies,** tax breaks (e.g., investment allowances),
or improved infrastructure can directly encourage investment.

**Disincentives:** High corporation tax, excessive red tape, or
political uncertainty can deter it.

E. Access to Credit

- Even with low interest rates, a **credit crunch** (where banks are
  unwilling to lend) can severely restrict investment, especially for
  SMEs.

3\. Impact of Investment

Short-run: Increases Aggregate Demand (AD).

**Long-run:** Increases the economy's **productive potential** (shifts
Long-Run Aggregate Supply - LRAS), promoting non-inflationary economic
growth.

Key Exam Tips:

**Distinguish:** Ensure you can differentiate
between **gross** and **net** investment and explain their significance
for economic growth.

**Link Concepts:** Connect investment to the **business cycle**—it is
highly **pro-cyclical** (falls in recessions, rises in booms). Also link
it to **interest rates set by the Bank of England**.

**Evaluation:** Investment depends on **expected future returns**, not
just current profits. Confidence can be fragile. Policies to boost
investment (e.g., tax cuts) may fail if business confidence is low.

**Application:** Use the **accelerator theory** to explain why
a *change* in the growth rate of GDP is more important for investment
than the level of growth itself.
