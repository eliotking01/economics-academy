Theme 4.4.2: Market Failure in the Financial Sector

1\. Causes of Market Failure

Financial markets are prone to severe failures with widespread
consequences (e.g., the 2008 crisis).

A. Asymmetric Information

**The Problem:** Sellers (banks, brokers) have **much more
information** than buyers (consumers, investors) about the complexity
and true risk of financial products.

**Example:** Banks selling high-risk **mortgage-backed
securities** before 2008, while buyers were unaware of the underlying
risk. This leads to **misallocation of capital** and systemic risk.

B. Moral Hazard

**The Problem:** When an agent takes excessive risks because they **do
not bear the full cost** of failure. In finance, this is exacerbated by
the belief that certain institutions are **"too big to fail"** and will
be bailed out by the government.

**Example:** Banks engaging in risky trading, knowing a potential
taxpayer bailout will protect them from total loss.

C. Speculation and Market Bubbles

**The Problem:** Excessive speculation can drive asset prices (e.g.,
houses, stocks) far above their fundamental value,
creating **unsustainable bubbles**. When the bubble bursts, it causes
crashes, wealth destruction, and recession.

**Example:** The **dot-com bubble** (1990s) and the **housing
bubble** (pre-2008).

D. Externalities

**The Problem:** The actions of financial institutions can
impose **massive costs on third parties** (the whole economy) not
involved in the original transaction.

**Example:** The collapse of a major bank can trigger a **credit
crunch**, causing innocent businesses to fail and unemployment to rise
globally.

E. Market Rigging and Fraud

**The Problem:** Collusion or manipulation of key benchmarks (e.g.,
LIBOR, foreign exchange rates) distorts prices, undermines confidence,
and constitutes a severe market failure.

2\. Consequences of Market Failure

**Systemic Risk:** Failure can spread through the entire financial
system, causing **financial crises**.

**Loss of Confidence:** Undermines trust in the banking system, leading
to **bank runs**.

**Real Economic Damage:** Triggers **recessions**, unemployment, and
loss of output.

**Inequity:** Often results in taxpayers bearing the cost of bailing out
private institutions.

Key Exam Tips:

**The 2008 Crisis:** This is the **definitive case study**. Use it to
illustrate **asymmetric information** (complex toxic assets), **moral
hazard** (expectation of bailouts), **speculation** (housing bubble),
and **huge negative externalities** (global recession).

**Justification for Intervention:** These failures provide a powerful
rationale for **strict government regulation** (e.g., capital
requirements, consumer protection, stress tests) and for regulatory
bodies like the **Bank of England's Prudential Regulation Authority
(PRA)**.

**Evaluation:** Regulating complex financial markets is challenging due
to **regulatory capture** and **innovation** (new products can outpace
rules). The trade-off is
between **stability** and **efficiency/innovation**.
