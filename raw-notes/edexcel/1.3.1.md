Theme 1.3.1: Types of Market Failure

1\. Understanding Market Failure

A **market failure** occurs when the free market, through the price
mechanism, fails to allocate scarce resources efficiently. This results
in a **loss of allocative efficiency** and a misallocation of resources
from society's point of view. Outcomes can include over-provision or
under-provision of goods and services, leading to issues like
inequality, pollution, or a lack of essential services.

2\. Types of Market Failure

**A. Externalities**\
Externalities are **spillover effects** on third parties not involved in
the original transaction. They are not reflected in market prices.

**Negative Externality:** A harmful spillover effect.

**Consumption:** e.g., smoking (passive smoking, public health costs).

**Production:** e.g., factory pollution (health and environmental
damage).

**Positive Externality:** A beneficial spillover effect.

**Consumption:** e.g., education (creates a more skilled, productive
society).

**Production:** e.g., beekeeping (pollination benefits nearby farms).

Without intervention, markets **over-provide** goods with negative
externalities and **under-provide** goods with positive externalities.

**B. Public Goods**\
These are goods which would be **under-provided or not provided at
all** by the free market due to two key characteristics:

**Non-excludability:** It is impossible to prevent non-payers from
consuming the good.

**Non-rivalry:** One person's consumption does not reduce the amount
available for others.

**Examples:** National defence, street lighting, public parks. Because
firms cannot charge users directly, they have no profit incentive to
supply them, leading to **market failure**. They are typically provided
by the government.

**C. Information Gaps**\
Markets rely on consumers and producers having good information to make
rational choices. **Asymmetric information** occurs when one party in a
transaction has more or better information than the other, distorting
the market outcome.

- **Examples:** A seller knowing a used car is faulty (a **'lemons'
  problem**), complex financial products, or the long-term health
  impacts of certain foods.

- **Result:** Poor choices are made (e.g., over-consumption of harmful
  goods, under-consumption of beneficial ones), leading to a
  misallocation of resources.

Key Exam Tips:

- **Core Definitions:** You must be able to define **market
  failure** and **allocative efficiency**.

- **Classify Examples:** Be prepared to identify and categorise
  different types of market failure (e.g., "Noise pollution from a
  nightclub is a **negative externality of consumption**").

- **Public Goods Test:** To identify a public good, check
  for **both** non-excludability **and** non-rivalry. If it lacks one,
  it is a **quasi-public good** (e.g., a toll road).

- **Evaluation:** Recognise that while market failure justifies
  government intervention, government failure is also possible. The
  existence of a market failure does not automatically mean government
  action will improve the situation.
