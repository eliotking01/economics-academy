Theme 2.2.2: Consumption (C)

1\. The Primary Influence: Disposable Income

Consumption is spending by households on goods and services. It is the
largest component of Aggregate Demand (AD) in most economies.

**Disposable Income (Yd)** is the key determinant: **Consumption =
f(Yd)**. This is income after direct taxes (e.g., income tax) and after
adding government transfer payments (e.g., benefits).

A rise in disposable income leads to a rise in consumption, and vice
versa.

2\. The Savings Relationship

Disposable income is split between Consumption (C) and Savings (S).
Therefore, Yd = C + S.

There is an **inverse relationship** between the **savings
ratio** (savings as a % of income) and consumption. Higher savings mean
lower consumption in the short run.

3\. Other Key Influences on Consumption

A. Interest Rates

- Higher interest rates:

  - Increase the incentive to **save**, reducing consumption.

  - Increase the cost of **borrowing** (e.g., for loans, mortgages),
    reducing consumption.

  - Increase monthly debt repayments, reducing disposable income for
    other spending.

- **Lower interest rates** have the opposite effect, stimulating
  consumption.

B. Consumer Confidence

High confidence (e.g., during strong growth, low unemployment)
encourages spending and borrowing, and discourages saving.

Low confidence (e.g., during a recession, rising unemployment) increases
precautionary saving and reduces consumption on non-essentials.

C. Wealth Effects

A rise in household **wealth** (e.g., from rising house prices or share
prices) creates a **positive wealth effect**. Households feel richer and
more secure, increasing their consumption (and willingness to borrow).

A fall in wealth creates a **negative wealth effect**, reducing
consumption.

D. Distribution of Income

Changes in how income is distributed affect total consumption.
Lower-income households have a **higher marginal propensity to consume
(MPC)**. Policies that increase their income (e.g., higher minimum wage)
may boost total consumption more than policies benefiting higher
earners.

Key Exam Tips:

- **Link to AD:** Consumption is **C** in **AD = C + I + G + (X-M)**. A
  change in any influence above will shift the AD curve.

- **Distinguish Income vs. Wealth:** **Income** is a flow (e.g.,
  salary). **Wealth** is a stock (e.g., value of house, savings). Both
  affect consumption, but through slightly different mechanisms.

- **Evaluation:** The impact of a change (e.g., a tax cut) depends on:

  - Whether it is seen as **temporary or permanent** (permanent changes
    have a bigger effect).

  - Consumer expectations and confidence at the time.

  - The **MPC** of those who receive the income.

- **Real-World Application:** Use recent economic events to illustrate
  these influences (e.g., the consumption surge after lockdowns due to
  pent-up demand and savings; the squeeze from rising interest rates in
  2023/24).
