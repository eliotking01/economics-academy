Theme 2.4.2: Injections & Withdrawals

1\. Recap: The Extended Circular Flow

The circular flow model expands to include
the **Government**, **Financial Sector**, and **Foreign Trade**. This
creates flows of money that can **increase** or **decrease** the size of
the national income flow.

2\. Injections (J) – Adding Money to the Flow

Injections are additions of **new spending** into the circular flow,
increasing aggregate demand and the size of the economy.

**Investment (I):** Spending by **firms** on capital goods (e.g., new
machinery, factories). *Source: Financial Sector.*

**Government Spending (G):** Spending by the **public sector** on
state-provided goods/services (e.g., NHS, roads, education). *Source:
Government.*

**Exports (X):** Revenue from **foreign buyers** purchasing domestically
produced goods/services. *Source: International Trade.*

3\. Withdrawals/Leakages (W) – Removing Money from the Flow

Withdrawals are incomes **not spent** on domestic output, reducing the
size of the circular flow.

**Savings (S):** Household income **not spent**, but placed in
banks/pensions. *Destination: Financial Sector.*

**Taxation (T):** Income paid to the **government** (e.g., income tax,
VAT). *Destination: Government.*

**Imports (M):** Spending on **goods/services produced
abroad**. Destination: International Trade.

4\. Equilibrium and Economic Change

**Equilibrium Condition:** The circular flow is stable (national income
is constant) when:\
**Total Injections = Total Withdrawals** → **I + G + X = S + T + M**

Economic Growth: Occurs when Injections \> Withdrawals (J \> W). The
flow of income expands, increasing real GDP.

Economic Decline/Recession: Occurs when Withdrawals \> Injections (W \>
J). The flow of income contracts, decreasing real GDP.

5\. The Multiplier Effect

**Concept:** An initial **change in injection** (e.g., a rise in G or I)
leads to a **larger final increase** in national income.

**Process:** The injection becomes someone's income (e.g., a builder's
wage). They **spend a proportion** of it (their **Marginal Propensity to
Consume - MPC**), which becomes income for others, who also spend a
proportion, and so on.

**Result:** The total rise in national income is a **multiple** of the
initial injection.

6\. How Changes in Economic Factors Affect the Flow

Changes in economic conditions alter injections and withdrawals,
shifting the equilibrium.

|  |  |  |
|----|----|----|
| Change in Factor | Effect on Injections/Withdrawals | Likely Impact on National Income |
| Interest Rates ↑ | Saving (W) ↑, Investment (J) ↓, Consumption ↓ (may reduce M). | W likely ↑ relative to J → Contractionary. |
| Government Spending ↑ | G (J) ↑. | **J** ↑ → Expansionary (Multiplier effect). |
| Exchange Rate ↓ (Depreciation) | **X (J)** ↑ (exports cheaper), **M (W)** ↓ (imports dearer). | **J** ↑**, W** ↓ → Expansionary. |
| Consumer Confidence ↑ | **Consumption** ↑ (may increase M), potentially **Investment (J)** ↑. | Ambiguous, but likely expansionary. |
