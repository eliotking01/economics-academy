Theme 2.6.1: Possible Macroeconomic Objectives

Governments typically aim for a mix of these objectives, which often
involve **trade-offs**.

1\. Economic Growth

**Aim:** Achieve **sustainable** growth (e.g., 2-3% in the UK).

**Why?** Increases incomes, living standards, employment, and tax
revenues. It is a key measure of economic success.

2\. Low Unemployment

**Aim:** Achieve a rate close to **full employment** (e.g., 4-5% in the
UK, accounting for frictional unemployment).

**Why?** Maximises output, reduces poverty and inequality, and improves
social welfare.

3\. Low and Stable Inflation

**Aim:** A **low, stable** rate (e.g., CPI target of 2% in the UK).

**Why?** Provides certainty for consumers and firms, protects savings,
maintains international competitiveness, and avoids the costs of
hyperinflation or deflation.

4\. Balance of Payments Equilibrium on the Current Account

**Aim:** A sustainable position, ideally a small surplus or deficit.

**Why?** Persistent large deficits imply a country is living beyond its
means and reliant on foreign borrowing. Large surpluses can cause
international tension.

5\. Balanced Government Budget (Fiscal Balance)

**Aim:** Balance revenue and expenditure over the economic cycle.

**Why?** To control **national debt**. High deficits can increase debt
servicing costs, reduce confidence, and limit future policy flexibility.

6\. Greater Income Equality

**Aim:** Reduce inequality (measured by the **Gini coefficient**) to
socially acceptable levels.

**Why?** Promotes social cohesion, can boost economic growth by
increasing consumption, and is seen as fair. Complete equality is not
desirable as it removes incentives.

7\. Environmental Protection

**Aim:** Achieve sustainability and reduce negative externalities (e.g.,
UK's net-zero targets).

**Why?** To ensure long-term economic viability and improve quality of
life.

Key Exam Tips:

**Trade-offs are Central:** Objectives often conflict (e.g., rapid
growth may cause inflation and a trade deficit; reducing inequality may
dampen incentives).

**Policy Conflicts:** A policy to fix one objective can worsen another
(e.g., raising interest rates to curb inflation may increase
unemployment and slow growth).

**Priorities Change:** The relative importance of objectives shifts with
circumstances (e.g., during a deep recession, growth and unemployment
take priority over a balanced budget).

**Link to Policies:** Each objective has associated demand-side
(fiscal/monetary) and supply-side policies. You must be able to suggest
appropriate policies and evaluate their likely success and conflicts.
