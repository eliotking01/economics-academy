Theme 4.3.2: Factors Influencing Growth and Development

1\. Economic Factors

**Primary Product Dependency:** Over-reliance on commodities (e.g., oil,
copper) causes problems:

Low income elasticity of demand limits revenue growth.

**Price volatility** leads to unstable incomes and GDP.

**Low value-added** compared to manufactured goods.

**Savings Gap (Harrod-Domar Model):** Low savings → low investment → low
growth. A cycle many developing countries struggle to
escape. **Criticism:** Model is simplistic, ignores productivity, human
capital, and institutions.

**Foreign Currency Gap:** Lack of foreign exchange (from weak exports or
high debt repayments) limits ability to import essential capital goods
and technology.

**Capital Flight:** Rapid outflow of money/assets due to instability,
reducing funds for domestic investment.

**Infrastructure Deficit:** Poor transport, energy, and communications
networks raise business costs and deter investment.

**Access to Credit & Banking:** Lack of financial systems prevents
savings mobilisation and investment in SMEs.

**Education & Skills (Human Capital):** Low levels reduce productivity
and ability to adopt new technologies.

**Demographic Factors:** A high **dependency ratio** (many young/old per
worker) reduces savings potential.

2\. Non-Economic / Institutional Factors

- **Corruption:** Diverts resources from productive investment, distorts
  decision-making, and increases costs.

- **Poor Governance & Political Instability:** Creates policy
  uncertainty, deters foreign investment (FDI), and leads to inefficient
  resource use.

- **Conflict & War:** Destroys capital, disrupts production, and sets
  back development by decades.

- **Absence of Property Rights:** Undermines investment incentives
  (people won't improve assets they don't own) and limits use of assets
  as collateral for loans.

- **Geography:** Being landlocked or having difficult terrain increases
  transport costs, reducing trade competitiveness.

3\. The Relationship Between Growth and Development

- **Growth is usually necessary but not sufficient for development.** An
  economy can grow (rising GDP) without development if benefits are not
  widely shared or if growth causes environmental/social harm.

- Development requires policies that ensure growth
  is: Inclusive (reduces
  poverty/inequality), sustainable (environmentally),
  and broad-based (diversified economy).

Key Exam Tips:

- **Interconnected Factors:** These factors are deeply linked (e.g.,
  corruption leads to poor governance, which deters investment,
  worsening the savings gap).

- **Two-Way Causation:** Development can also influence factors (e.g.,
  better education reduces corruption over time).

- **Evaluation:** A country's development path is unique. There is no
  single formula. **Institutional quality** (good governance, rule of
  law) is increasingly seen as a fundamental prerequisite.

- **Policy Implications:** Solutions must address root causes. For a
  commodity-dependent country, the key
  is **diversification** and **investment in human capital**. For one
  suffering capital flight, **political stability** and **property
  rights** are essential.
