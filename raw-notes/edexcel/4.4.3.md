Theme 4.4.3: Role of Central Banks

1\. Key Functions of a Central Bank (e.g., Bank of England)

Central banks are responsible for maintaining **monetary and financial
stability**.

**Implementation of Monetary Policy:** The primary role. They set
the **base interest rate** and use other tools (e.g., quantitative
easing) to control inflation and support other macroeconomic objectives.

**Banker to the Government:** Manages the government's bank accounts,
processes payments (e.g., public sector wages), and manages the national
debt.

**Banker to the Banks (Lender of Last Resort):** Provides emergency
loans to commercial banks facing short-term liquidity crises to prevent
bank runs and maintain confidence in the financial system.

**Regulation of the Banking System:** Sets and enforces rules
(e.g., **capital and liquidity requirements**) to ensure banks are
stable, protect depositors, and reduce systemic risk. In the UK, this is
done by the **Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA)**, part of the Bank
of England.

**Issue of Banknotes:** Has a monopoly on issuing legal tender.

2\. Importance for Stability

By performing these functions, central banks aim to:

Ensure **price stability** (low and stable inflation).

Maintain confidence in the currency and the payment system.

Prevent and manage **financial crises**.

Key Exam Tips:

- **Independence:** A key concept is **operational independence** (e.g.,
  the Bank of England's Monetary Policy Committee sets interest rates
  free from government influence). This is intended to prevent
  politically-motivated decisions that could cause high inflation.

- **Link to 2008 Crisis:** The crisis highlighted the critical
  importance of the **lender of last resort** function and led to a
  major expansion of the **regulatory role** (with stricter rules on
  banks).

- **Policy Tools:** Be able to distinguish between **monetary policy
  tools** (interest rates, QE) and **regulatory/prudential
  tools** (capital ratios, stress tests). Both are used to manage the
  economy but target different things.

- **Evaluation:** Central banks face challenges: balancing objectives
  (inflation vs. growth), the effectiveness of tools at the **zero lower
  bound**, and the risk that prolonged intervention (like QE) can create
  asset bubbles or inequality.
