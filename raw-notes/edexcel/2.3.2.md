Theme 2.3.2: Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS)

1\. The SRAS Curve: A Recap

**Definition:** The total planned output of goods/services in an economy
at a given price level, assuming at least one factor of production
(e.g., capital, technology) is fixed.

**The Curve:** It is **upward sloping** because, in the short run,
rising output leads to higher unit costs (e.g., overtime pay), requiring
a higher price to incentivise production.

Y-axis and Real GDP on the X-axis.

2\. Shifts in the SRAS Curve

A shift of the entire SRAS curve is caused by a change in the **costs of
production** for businesses across the economy. This changes the level
of output firms are willing to supply **at every given price level**.

- A curve to the right labelled **SRAS2 (Increase in AS)**.

- A curve to the left labelled **SRAS3 (Decrease in AS)**.

4\. Exam Focus: Key Takeaways

- **Draw** the SRAS curve and show shifts to the left and right.

- **Explain** that SRAS shifts are caused by changes in **costs of
  production**.

- **Analyse** the impact of specific events using a clear chain of
  reasoning. For example:

  - *Event:* A sharp fall in the global price of oil.

  - *Analysis:* This lowers energy and transport costs for most firms →
    decreases costs of production → SRAS increases (shifts right) →
    leads to a higher level of real output at each price level.

- **Link** this topic to causes of **cost-push inflation** (a leftward
  shift in SRAS increases the price level).
