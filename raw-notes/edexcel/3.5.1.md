Theme 3.5.1: Demand for Labour

1\. Demand for Labour is a Derived Demand

The demand for labour is not wanted for itself but for what it can
produce.

It is derived from the demand for the goods and services that labour
helps to produce.

**Example:** Demand for builders is derived from the demand for new
houses.

2\. The Marginal Revenue Product (MRP) Theory

Firms base their demand for labour on the **Marginal Revenue Product
(MRP)** of workers.

MRP = Marginal Physical Product (MPP) × Marginal Revenue (MR)

**MPP:** The **extra output** produced by one more worker.

**MR:** The **extra revenue** from selling that extra output.

MRP is the demand curve for labour (DL). It is downward sloping due to
the law of diminishing returns.

3\. Profit-Maximising Employment Level

A profit-maximising firm will hire workers up to the point where:\
**Marginal Revenue Product (MRP) = Marginal Cost of Labour (MCL)**

- In a competitive labour market, the MCL is the market wage rate (W).

- Therefore, the firm hires where **MRP = W**.

4\. Factors that Shift the Demand for Labour Curve

A change in any factor that affects the **MRP of labour** will shift the
demand curve.

|  |  |  |
|----|----|----|
| Factor | Effect on Demand for Labour (Shift) | Reason |
| ↑ Demand for the Final Product | Increase (Shift Right) | ↑ Price (MR) → ↑ MRP of workers. |
| ↑ Productivity of Labour (MPP) | Increase (Shift Right) | e.g., due to better training, technology, or management. |
| ↑ Price of Capital | Increase (Shift Right) | Labour becomes **relatively cheaper** compared to capital (substitution effect). |
| ↓ Price of Capital | Decrease (Shift Left) | Capital becomes **relatively cheaper**, firms may substitute capital for labour. |
| ↓ Wages (a change in the price of labour itself) | **Movement along** the demand curve (extension in QD of labour). | Not a shift. This is a change in quantity demanded. |

5\. Elasticity of Demand for Labour

The responsiveness of the quantity of labour demanded to a change in the
wage rate. It is influenced by:

**PED of the Final Product:** More **elastic** product demand →
more **elastic** labour demand (a wage rise cannot be easily passed on
in higher prices).

**Ease of Substituting Labour:** If capital can easily replace labour,
demand is more **elastic**.

Proportion of Labour Costs to Total Costs: If labour is a high % of
costs, demand is more elastic.

**Time Period:** More **elastic** in the **long run** (firms can
reorganise production).
