Theme 2.4.3: Equilibrium Levels of Real National Output

1\. Macroeconomic Equilibrium

Short-Run Equilibrium: Occurs where Aggregate Demand (AD) = Short-Run
Aggregate Supply (SRAS). This determines the actual Price Level
(AP) and Real National Output (Y).

Long-Run Equilibrium (Classical View): Occurs where AD = SRAS = LRAS at
the full employment level of output (Yfe).

2\. The Two Views of Long-Run Equilibrium

\*(Hand-draw Diagram 1a & 1b: Classical vs. Keynesian LRAS with AD
intersection)\*

a\) Classical Equilibrium

**LRAS is vertical** at **Yfe** (full employment output).

**Equilibrium:** AD intersects SRAS at the **LRAS** curve.

**Belief:** The economy **always self-corrects** to Yfe in the long run.
Changes in AD only affect the **price level**, not long-run output.

b\) Keynesian Equilibrium

**LRAS is L-shaped** (horizontal, then upward-sloping, then vertical at
Yfe).

**Equilibrium:** Can occur **at any point** along the LRAS curve.

**Belief:** The economy can be **stuck in equilibrium below Yfe** (e.g.,
in a recession). Only an increase in AD can raise output in this
situation without causing inflation.

6\. Output Gaps

**Negative Output Gap (Recessionary Gap):** When actual output (**Y**)
is **less than** potential output (**Yfe**). Resources (especially
labour) are underutilised.

**Positive Output Gap (Inflationary Gap):** When actual output (**Y**)
is **greater than** Yfe. The economy is overheating, using resources
unsustainably (e.g., excessive overtime), leading to inflationary
pressure.

7\. Exam Focus: Key Takeaways

**Draw and explain** equilibrium using **both** Classical and Keynesian
LRAS.

**Analyse** the different effects of an **increase in AD** depending on
the economic model and the economy's starting position (spare capacity
vs. full capacity).

**Show and explain** how an **increase in LRAS** represents long-run
economic growth.

**Identify** negative and positive output gaps on diagrams.

**Evaluate** the key policy implication: Keynesians advocate
for **demand-side intervention** (e.g., fiscal policy) to close a
recessionary gap, while Classical economists believe the market will
self-correct.
