Theme 1.2.10: Alternative Views of Consumer Behaviour

1\. Challenging the Rational Consumer

Classical economics assumes consumers are **rational**, making logical
choices to **maximise utility** based on a full cost-benefit analysis.\
**Behavioural economics** challenges this, arguing that consumers are
often **irrational** and their choices are systematically influenced by
psychological biases and rules of thumb.

2\. Key Influences on Irrational Behaviour

A. Social Influences & Herding

Consumers are influenced by the behaviour of others, leading
to **herding** (following the crowd).

**Marketing and advertising** exploit this, using celebrity endorsements
and social proof to create emotional, rather than purely rational,
purchasing decisions (e.g., buying a branded product over a chemically
identical generic).

B. Habit & Inertia

- Due to the sheer number of decisions, consumers rely
  on **habits** and **rules of thumb** (mental shortcuts) to save time
  and effort.

- This leads to **consumer inertia**, where people stick with familiar
  choices out of convenience, even if better alternatives exist (e.g.,
  always shopping at the same supermarket).

- Firms exploit this through product placement (e.g., impulse buys at
  checkouts) and by making it easy to repeat purchases.

C. Bounded Rationality & Weakness in Computation

- Consumers suffer from **bounded rationality**: they make decisions
  with limited information, time, and cognitive ability.

- The complexity of choice can overwhelm, making a full utility
  calculation impossible.

- Firms can manipulate this by making price comparisons difficult or by
  strategically placing products (e.g., high-margin items at eye level)
  to shortcut rational evaluation.

3\. Implications for Economic Theory

These insights explain why consumers may not respond to price signals as
predictably as simple demand models suggest. They provide a more
realistic foundation for understanding **market failures** and for
designing policies (e.g., **'nudges'**) that account for actual human
behaviour.

Key Exam Tips:

**Contrast:** Clearly distinguish the classical assumption of
a **rational utility-maximiser** from the behavioural view of
an **irrational, bias-prone decision-maker**.

Apply Concepts: Use specific terms like bounded rationality, herding,
inertia, and rule of thumb in your explanations.

**Real-World Examples:** Always support your points with examples (e.g.,
brand loyalty, subscription auto-renewals, impulse buys).

**Evaluation:** This topic is perfect for evaluating the limitations of
traditional economic models and for discussing how governments and firms
might use behavioural insights.
