Theme 1.4.1: Government Intervention in Markets

1\. Reasons for Government Intervention

Governments intervene in mixed economies to:

**Correct Market Failure:** Achieve a more socially efficient allocation
of resources (e.g., address externalities).

**Earn Government Revenue:** Fund public services (via taxes, sale of
licenses).

**Promote Equity (Fairness):** Reduce inequality and support poorer
households.

**Support Firms:** Help key industries remain competitive globally.

2\. Indirect Taxation

- **Purpose:** To reduce consumption of demerit goods, reduce negative
  externalities, and raise revenue.

- Types:

  - **Specific Tax:** Fixed amount per unit (parallel shift of S curve).

  - **Ad Valorem Tax:** Percentage of price (S curve pivots upward,
    diverging from original).

but **diverges upward** (steeper).

Mark the new equilibrium **P2, Q2**.

Label the **government tax revenue** area between S1, S2, and the new
price line.

Show the consumer and producer incidence.

3\. Subsidies

**Purpose:** To increase consumption/production of merit goods,
encourage positive externalities, and support producers.

**Effect:** Lowers costs for producers, shifting the **supply curve to
the right**.

\*(Hand-draw Diagram 2: Subsidy to Correct Positive Externality)\*

Draw a market with S1 (MPC), D (MPB), and an **MSB curve above D**.

Mark the market failure at **Qm** (where MPB=MPC) and the social optimum
at **Qopt** (where MSB=MSC).

Draw **S2** (MPC + subsidy) parallel and below S1.

Show the new equilibrium closer to Qopt, with **lower consumer price
(Pc)** and **higher producer revenue (Pp)**.

Shade the **government cost** of the subsidy and the **reduced welfare
loss**.

4\. Maximum Prices (Price Ceilings)

**Purpose:** To make essential goods/services (e.g., food, rent)
affordable for consumers.

Mechanism: A legal maximum price set below the equilibrium.

2.  Draw a horizontal **Pmax line below Pe**.

3.  At Pmax, label **QS** (on S curve) and **QD** (on D curve).

4.  Highlight the resulting excess demand (shortage = QD - QS).

5.  **Consequences:** Shortages, rationing, black markets, reduced
    quality/investment.

6\. Other Intervention Methods

**Tradeable Pollution Permits:** A cap-and-trade system where firms can
buy/sell permits to pollute. Creates a market incentive to reduce
pollution.

**State Provision:** Government directly supplies public goods (e.g.,
street lighting, defence) that the free market would not provide due to
the **free-rider problem**.

**Provision of Information:** Corrects information gaps and asymmetric
information (e.g., food labelling, consumer advice).

**Regulation:** Laws and rules to limit harmful activities (e.g.,
emissions standards, health and safety laws). Enforced by regulators
with fines.

7\. Exam Focus: Key Takeaways

**Understand the rationale** for each type of intervention.

Draw, label, and analyse the four key diagrams: Ad Valorem Tax, Subsidy,
Max Price, Min Price.

**Analyse consequences:** Always consider the impact on **consumers,
producers, the government, and society** (e.g., shortages, surpluses,
welfare loss, revenue, cost).

**Evaluate effectiveness:** For taxes/subsidies, consider **PED**. For
max/min prices, discuss unintended consequences (e.g., black markets,
government cost of buying surpluses).

**Link interventions to market failure:** Explain *how* a tax corrects a
negative externality or *how* a subsidy corrects a positive externality.
