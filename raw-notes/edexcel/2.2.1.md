Theme 2.2.1: Aggregate Demand (AD)

1\. What is Aggregate Demand?

**Definition:** The **total planned expenditure** on a country's goods
and services at a given average price level in a given time period.

Formula (Expenditure Approach): AD = C + I + G + (X-M)

**C** = **Consumption** (Household spending)

**I** = **Investment** (Spending by firms on capital goods)

**G** = **Government Spending** (On state-provided
goods/services. **Excludes** transfer payments like benefits)

**(X-M)** = **Net Exports** (Exports minus Imports)

2\. The Components of AD & Their Relative Importance (UK Context)

- Consumption (C): ~60% of AD. The largest and most stable component.

- Investment (I): ~14% of AD. The most volatile component.

- Government Spending (G): ~25% of AD.

- **Net Exports (X-M):** Typically a small, **often
  negative** percentage (~1% or less).

- **Implication:** A 1% change in **Consumption** has a far greater
  impact on economic growth than a 1% change in Net Exports.

3\. The Aggregate Demand Curve

\*(Hand-draw Diagram 1: The AD Curve)\*

Draw axes: Y-axis = Average Price Level, X-axis = Real GDP (National
Output).

Draw a **downward-sloping** AD curve. Label it **AD**.

Why is the AD curve downward sloping?

**The Wealth Effect:** A higher price level erodes the real value of
savings/income, reducing consumption (C).

**The Interest Rate Effect:** A higher price level increases demand for
money, pushing up interest rates. This discourages investment (I) and
consumption on credit.

**The International Trade Effect:** A higher domestic price level makes
exports less competitive and imports cheaper, reducing net exports
(X-M).

4\. Movements vs. Shifts of the AD Curve

- A Movement Along the AD Curve:

  - **Cause:** A change in the **Average Price Level** (ceteris
    paribus).

  - This shows how the **quantity of real GDP demanded** changes with
    the price level.

\*(Hand-draw Diagram 2: Movement Along AD)\*\
On your AD curve, show a movement **up and left** labelled "Contraction
of AD" and a movement **down and right** labelled "Extension of AD".

- A Shift of the Entire AD Curve:

  - Cause: A change in any determinant of AD other than the price
    level (i.e., a change in C, I, G, or X-M).

  - This changes the total planned expenditure at every given price
    level.

5\. Determinants of AD (Causes of Shifts)

|  |  |  |
|----|----|----|
| Component | Factors that cause an **INCREASE** in AD (Shift Right) | Factors that cause a **DECREASE** in AD (Shift Left) |
| Consumption (C) | ↑ Disposable Income, ↓ Income Tax, ↓ Interest Rates, ↑ Consumer Confidence, ↑House Prices (Wealth Effect). | Opposite of left column. |
| Investment (I) | ↓ Interest Rates, ↑ Business Confidence/Profits, ↑ Demand for Exports, Technological Advancements. | Opposite of left column. |
| Government Spending (G) | ↑ Government Spending on services, infrastructure, public sector wages (Expansionary Fiscal Policy). | ↓ Government Spending (Contractionary Fiscal Policy). |
| Net Exports (X-M) | ↓ Exchange Rate, Strong Economic Growth in Trading Partners, ↓ Tariffs Abroad. | ↑ Exchange Rate, Recession in Trading Partners, ↑ Domestic Demand for Imports. |

6\. Exam Focus: Key Takeaways

- Know the AD formula (C+I+G+(X-M)) and the approximate UK weights.

- **Draw and explain** why the AD curve is downward sloping (the three
  effects).

- **Crucially distinguish** between a **movement along** the AD curve
  (caused by price level change) and a **shift** of the AD curve (caused
  by a change in its determinants).

- **Analyse** how changes in key economic variables (e.g., interest
  rates, exchange rates, taxes, confidence) affect the components of AD
  and thus shift the curve.
