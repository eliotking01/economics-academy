Theme 2.2.4: Government Expenditure (G)

1\. Definition and Role

Government expenditure (G) is spending by the public sector on goods,
services, and transfer payments. It is a key component of Aggregate
Demand (AD = C + I + G + (X-M)) and is used to achieve macroeconomic and
microeconomic objectives.

2\. Key Influences on Government Spending

**A. The Economic Cycle (Automatic Stabilisers)**\
Government spending changes **automatically** over the business cycle,
helping to stabilise the economy:

**In a Recession/Weak Economy:** Spending **increases** due to higher
unemployment benefits and other welfare payments. Tax revenues fall.
This **automatic fiscal expansion** helps to support AD.

**In a Boom/Strong Economy:** Spending **decreases** as welfare claims
fall. Tax revenues rise. This **automatic fiscal contraction** helps to
cool the economy.

B. Fiscal Policy Discretion (Deliberate Policy)\
Governments make discretionary changes to spending to meet policy aims:

**Expansionary Fiscal Policy:** **Increasing G** (e.g., on
infrastructure, education) to boost AD, growth, and employment.

**Contractionary Fiscal Policy:** **Decreasing G** to reduce AD, control
inflation, or reduce a budget deficit.

**C. Political and Social Priorities**\
Spending reflects government priorities, which can shift:

**Long-term Supply-Side Policies:** Investment in infrastructure,
education, and healthcare to increase productive potential (LRAS).

**Political Promises:** Manifesto commitments (e.g., increasing defence
spending, building new hospitals).

**Demographic Changes:** An ageing population increases spending on
pensions and healthcare.

3\. Impact of Changes in Government Spending

**On AD:** An increase in G directly increases AD, potentially raising
real GDP, employment, and inflation (depending on spare capacity).

**On LRAS:** Spending on infrastructure, education, and technology can
increase the economy's productive capacity, shifting LRAS to the right.

**On the Budget Deficit:** Increased spending, unless matched by higher
taxes, worsens the government's budget balance, potentially increasing
national debt.

Key Exam Tips:

- **Automatic vs. Discretionary:** A crucial distinction. **Automatic
  stabilisers** operate without new laws (e.g., rising benefits in a
  downturn). **Discretionary policy** involves active new decisions
  (e.g., a stimulus package).

- **Link to Objectives:** Connect spending to goals: higher G
  for **growth/employment**; lower G for **controlling inflation/deficit
  reduction**.

- **Evaluation:** The effectiveness of changing G depends on:

  - The **multiplier effect** (how much total AD increases from an
    initial injection).

  - The **state of the economy** (crowding out is more likely at full
    capacity).

  - **Confidence effects** (will firms invest more if they see
    government spending?).

- **Trade-offs:** There is often a **trade-off** between using G to
  manage AD in the short run and the long-run goal of maintaining
  sustainable public finances.
