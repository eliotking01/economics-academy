Theme 2.6.3: Supply-Side Policies

1\. What are Supply-Side Policies?

Policies that aim to **increase the productive potential** of the
economy by shifting the **Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS)** curve to
the right. This achieves **long-term economic growth**.

2\. Two Approaches

**Market-Based:** Aim to **increase efficiency** by **removing market
distortions** (e.g., reducing regulation, weakening trade unions). Focus
on incentives.

**Interventionist:** Involve **government spending/intervention** to
correct market failure (e.g., state-funded education, infrastructure).

3\. Key Aims & Policy Examples

|  |  |  |
|----|----|----|
| Aim | Market-Based Policy Example | Interventionist Policy Example |
| Increase Incentives | Reduce income/corporation tax rates. Reform welfare to encourage work. | \- |
| Promote Competition | Privatisation, deregulation, trade liberalisation. | Subsidies for start-ups, R&D grants. |
| Reform Labour Market | Reduce trade union power, freeze/min. wage. | Gov't funding for retraining, improved job centres. |
| Improve Labour Quality | \- | Increase spending on education & healthcare. |
| Improve Infrastructure | \- | **Government investment** in transport, broadband, energy. |

6\. Exam Focus: Key Takeaways

**Define** supply-side policies and their objective (to shift LRAS).

**Distinguish** between **market-based** and **interventionist** approaches
with examples.

**Draw** an LRAS shift on both Classical and Keynesian diagrams and
explain the outcome (↑ output, ↓ price level potential).

**Analyse** how a specific policy (e.g., higher education spending,
corporate tax cut) works to increase productive potential.

Evaluate their effectiveness, considering time lags, costs, and impact
on equity.
