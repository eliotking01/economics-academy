Theme 4.5.1: Public Expenditure

1\. Types of Public Expenditure

Current Expenditure: Day-to-day spending on public sector
wages (teachers, nurses) and consumption of goods/services (medicines,
utilities).

**Capital Expenditure:** Long-term **investment in infrastructure** and
assets (roads, hospitals, schools). This increases the
economy's **productive potential**.

**Transfer Payments:** Payments where **no good/service is received** in
return (e.g., pensions, unemployment benefits, subsidies).
They **redistribute income** but do not directly contribute to GDP.

2\. Reasons for Changing Size and Composition

**Economic Development:** As incomes rise, demand
for **income-elastic** public services (healthcare, education,
environment) increases.

**Demographic Change:** An **ageing population** increases spending on
pensions and healthcare.

**Changing Social Expectations:** Societies demand higher quality or new
types of public services (e.g., digital access, social care).

**Economic Shocks:** Recessions or crises (e.g., 2008, COVID-19) cause
spending to **rise automatically** (more welfare payments)
and **discretionarily** (stimulus packages).

3\. Significance of Public Expenditure as % of GDP

- **Indicates the Size of the State:** A higher % suggests greater
  government involvement in the economy.

- Impacts on Economic Performance:

  - **Potential Benefits:** Can improve **equity**, fund **public
    goods**, invest in **supply-side capacity** (LRAS), and stabilise
    the economy (automatic stabilisers).

  - **Potential Drawbacks:** High spending may require **higher taxes**,
    lead to **crowding out** of private investment, cause **government
    failure** (inefficiency), or create **fiscal deficits and debt**.

Key Exam Tips:

- **Distinguish Types:** Know the difference
  between **capital** (investment for future)
  and **current** (day-to-day) spending, and that **transfer
  payments** are not part of GDP.

- **Link to Objectives:** Public spending is a key tool for
  achieving **macroeconomic objectives** (e.g., growth via investment,
  equity via transfers, stability via automatic stabilisers).

- **Evaluation:** The **optimal size** of public expenditure is debated.
  It involves a **trade-off**:

  - **Equity vs. Efficiency:** High spending may promote fairness but
    could reduce incentives and efficiency.

  - **Short-run vs. Long-run:** Stimulus spending boosts AD short-term
    but high debt may harm long-term growth.

  - **Market Failure vs. Government Failure:** Spending corrects market
    failures but risks government inefficiency.

- **Context Matters:** The impact depends on **how effectively** the
  money is spent (quality of investment) and how it
  is **financed** (taxes vs. borrowing).
