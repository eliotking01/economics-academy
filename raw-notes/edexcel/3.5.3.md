Theme 3.5.2 (part 2): Wage Determination

1\. Wage Determination in a Competitive Labour Market

**Many firms** demand labour and **many workers** supply labour.

Both firms and workers are **wage takers**; they accept the **market
equilibrium wage**.

\*(Hand-draw Diagram 1: Competitive Labour Market Equilibrium)\*

Draw axes: Wage Rate (W) vs Quantity of Labour (Q).

Draw a downward-sloping Demand for Labour (DL) curve.

Draw an upward-sloping Supply of Labour (SL) curve.

Mark the intersection as equilibrium **We, Qe**.

2\. Wage Determination in a Non-Competitive Market: Monopsony

A **monopsony** exists when there is a **single or dominant buyer** of
labour (e.g., NHS, large employer in a town).

\*(Hand-draw Diagram 2: Monopsony Labour Market)\*

Draw axes: Wage (W) vs Quantity of Labour (Q).

Draw the upward-sloping **SL** curve (also the Average Cost of Labour,
ACL).

Draw the Marginal Cost of Labour (MCL) curve above and steeper than SL.

Draw the firm's **DL (MRP)** curve.

The monopsonist hires where **MCL = MRP** at quantity **Qm**.

To attract Qm workers, it pays wage **Wm** from the **SL curve**.

Mark the competitive outcome where **SL = DL** at **Wc, Qc**.

- Result: Wm \< Wc and Qm \< Qc. The monopsonist depresses wages and
  employment.

3\. Government Intervention: The National Minimum Wage (NMW)

A legally enforced **price floor** set above the market equilibrium
wage.

\*(Hand-draw Diagram 3: Impact of a Minimum Wage)\*

Draw a competitive market at **We, Qe**.

Impose a horizontal minimum wage line (Wmin) above We.

At Wmin, **quantity demanded (Qd)** is on the DL curve and **quantity
supplied (Qs)** is on the SL curve.

Label the excess supply (Qs - Qd) as potential real-wage unemployment.

- **Impact:** Raises wages for low-paid workers but may reduce
  employment if demand is elastic. The effect depends on
  the **level** of the NMW and the **PED of demand for labour**.

4\. Elasticity of Demand for Labour (PED)

The responsiveness of quantity of labour demanded to a change in the
wage rate.\
**Factors making PED more elastic:**

- Labour is a high % of total costs.

- Easy to substitute labour with capital.

- **High PED for the final product** (so a wage rise cannot be passed
  on).

- **Longer time period** (firms can reorganise).

5\. Elasticity of Supply of Labour (PES)

The responsiveness of quantity of labour supplied to a change in the
wage rate.

- **Elastic Supply:** Common for **low-skilled** jobs (easy entry/exit).

- **Inelastic Supply:** Common for **highly skilled** professions (long
  training, high barriers).

6\. Current Labour Market Issues & Policies (UK Context)

- **Issues:** Public sector pay disputes, skills shortages,
  cost-of-living crisis, geographical immobility (North-South divide).

- Policies to Tackle Immobility:

  - **Occupational:** Funding for **retraining**, apprenticeships, STEM
    education.

  - **Geographical:** **Relocation subsidies**, investment in regional
    infrastructure (Northern Powerhouse), affordable housing.

7\. Exam Focus: Key Takeaways

- Draw, label and analyse diagrams for competitive labour markets,
  a monopsony, and the impact of a national minimum wage.

- **Compare** wage and employment outcomes in competitive vs. monopsony
  markets.

- **Evaluate** the impact of a NMW, considering the role of **PED for
  labour**.

- **Explain** the factors influencing the **PED and PES of labour**.

- **Analyse** real-world labour market issues and evaluate government
