Theme 3.5.2: Supply of Labour

1\. The Supply of Labour Curve

Represents the number of workers willing and able to work at different
wage rates.

For an **individual**, the curve may **bend backwards** at very high
wages (income effect \> substitution effect). For a **market or
occupation**, it is usually **upward sloping**: higher wages incentivise
more people to enter that job.

2\. Factors that Shift the Supply of Labour Curve

A change in any factor **other than the wage** that affects the
willingness/ability to work in an occupation shifts the entire curve.

|  |  |  |
|----|----|----|
| Factor | Effect on Supply of Labour | Reason & Example |
| Changes in Demographics | Population ↑ → Supply ↑ (shift right) | More people of working age. |
| Changes in Net Migration | Net Immigration ↑ → Supply ↑ (shift right) | More workers enter the labour force. |
| Changes in Education/Training | Skills/Qualifications ↑ → Supply ↑ for skilled jobs (shift right) | More people qualify for the profession (e.g., more graduates). |
| Changes in Barriers to Entry | Barriers ↓ (e.g., easier exams) → Supply ↑ (shift right) | Easier to enter the profession. |
| Changes in Other Wages/Job Benefits | Wages in alternative jobs ↑ → Supply in this job ↓ (shift left) | Workers move to better-paid alternatives (opportunity cost). |
| Changes in Non-pecuniary Benefits | Improved working conditions → Supply ↑ (shift right) | Job becomes more attractive (e.g., flexible hours, job satisfaction). |
| Changes in Income Tax/ Benefits | **Income Tax** ↓ → **Supply may** ↑ (substitution effect). Complex due to income effect. | Higher net wage increases incentive to work. |

3\. Labour Mobility & Market Failure

Labour immobility is a key cause of market failure in labour markets,
leading to **structural unemployment** and skills shortages.

- **Geographical Immobility:** Workers' inability to move to areas where
  jobs are available.

  - **Causes:** High house prices/rent, family ties, lack of
    information, regional differences in living costs.

- **Occupational Immobility:** Workers' inability to move between
  jobs/industries due to skill mismatches.

  - **Causes:** Lack of transferable skills, need for specific
    qualifications/training, technological change.

Government Policies to Improve Mobility:

- **Occupational:** Subsidised training/retraining schemes, improved
  careers advice, funding for STEM education.

- **Geographical:** Relocation grants, investment in regional
  infrastructure (e.g., broadband, transport), affordable housing
  schemes.

4\. Elasticity of Supply of Labour

The responsiveness of the quantity of labour supplied to a change in the
wage rate.

- **Elastic Supply:** Quantity supplied changes significantly with a
  wage change (common for low-skilled jobs with few entry barriers).

- **Inelastic Supply:** Quantity supplied changes little (common for
  highly specialised professions requiring long training, e.g., doctors,
  pilots).

5\. Exam Focus: Key Takeaways

- **Draw** the upward-sloping supply of labour curve.

- Analyse factors that cause an increase or decrease in the supply of
  labour to a specific occupation (shifts in the curve).

- Define, distinguish between, and explain the causes of geographical
  and occupational immobility.

- **Explain** how labour immobility leads to **labour market
  failure** (structural unemployment).

- **Suggest** and **evaluate** government policies to improve labour
  mobility.
