Theme 4.1.6: Restrictions on Free Trade (Protectionism)

1\. Reasons for Protectionism

Governments restrict free trade to protect domestic industries, despite
the efficiency gains of comparative advantage.

|  |  |
|----|----|
| Reason | Explanation |
| Protect Infant Industries | Shield new, developing industries from established foreign competition until they mature. |
| Prevent Dumping | Stop foreign firms selling goods below cost to drive out domestic competition (anti-competitive). |
| Correct a Current Account Deficit | Reduce imports to improve the trade balance. |
| Protect Employment | Save jobs in declining (sunset) or strategically important industries (e.g., steel, agriculture). |
| Strategic & Security Reasons | Ensure self-sufficiency in key areas like energy, food, and defence. |
| Environmental/Labour Standards | Pressure countries with lower standards by restricting imports from them. |

2\. Methods of Protectionism

**a) Tariffs**\
A **tax on imports**. Increases the price of imported goods, making
domestic goods relatively cheaper.

(Hand-draw Diagram: The Impact of a Tariff)

Draw domestic demand (**D**) and supply (**S**) curves.

Draw a horizontal **World Supply (W)** curve below the domestic
equilibrium price. Label initial price **Pw**.

Show initial imports as the horizontal distance between **Qd** (on D)
and **Qs** (on S) at Pw.

Impose the tariff by shifting **W up** to **W+Tariff**.

Show the new, higher domestic price **Pw+T**.

Show that domestic quantity supplied increases (extension), domestic
quantity demanded falls (contraction), and imports shrink.

Impact: Government gains tariff revenue. Consumer surplus
falls. Producer surplus rises.

**b) Quotas**\
A **physical limit** on the quantity of a good that can be imported.

**c) Subsidies to Domestic Producers**\
Government payments that **lower production costs** for domestic firms,
allowing them to lower prices and compete with imports.

**d) Non-Tariff Barriers (NTBs)**\
Rules and regulations that hinder imports (e.g., **complex product
standards**, **health/safety rules**, **administrative red tape**).

3\. Impacts of Protectionist Policies

|  |  |  |
|----|----|----|
| Stakeholder | Impact of a **Tariff** / **Quota** | Impact of a **Subsidy** |
| Domestic Producers | **Benefit:** Higher prices/revenue, increased output, protected jobs. | **Benefit:** Lower costs, increased output & competitiveness. |
| Domestic Consumers | **Lose:** Higher prices, less choice, lower consumer surplus. | **May Benefit:** Lower prices, but cost is borne by taxpayers. |
| Government | Gains tariff revenue (tariff only). | Pays the subsidy cost (opportunity cost). |
| Foreign Producers | **Lose:** Reduced access to market, lower sales. | **Lose:** Face unfair competition from subsidised firms. |
| Efficiency | **Declines:** Resources allocated to less efficient domestic producers. **Global output falls.** | **Distorted:** May support inefficient firms. Risk of trade wars. |

4\. Evaluation of Protectionism

**Arguments For:** Can be justified for **infant
industries**, **national security**, or to prevent **unfair
practices** like dumping.

**Arguments Against:** **Higher prices for consumers**, **loss of
economic efficiency**, **retaliation and trade wars**, **less
innovation** due to reduced competition. Often protects **inefficient
firms**.
