Theme 3.4.7: Contestability

1\. What is a Contestable Market?

A market is contestable when there is freedom of entry and low costs of
exit. The threat of potential competition (not just actual competitors)
disciplines firms\' behaviour.

Key Distinction: Competition is about the number of firms in a market.
Contestability is about the ease of entry and exit.

2\. Key Characteristics of a Contestable Market

No barriers to entry or exit (or very low barriers). Sunk costs are
minimal or zero.

No competitive disadvantage for new entrants (same technology, access to
resources).

Perfect information is available to all firms (no proprietary
knowledge/patents).

\"Hit and Run\" Competition: New firms can enter quickly to
capture short-run supernormal profit, then exit easily when profit
returns to normal.

3\. Implications for Firms

Disciplined Behaviour: The threat of hit-and-run entry forces incumbent
firms to act more competitively, even if there are few actual
competitors.

Limit Pricing: Existing firms may set price below the profit-maximising
level (P = AC) to earn only normal profit. This removes the profit
signal that would attract new entrants.

Outcome: The more contestable the market, the more firm behaviour
resembles perfect competition (lower prices, higher output, allocative
efficiency).

5\. Sunk Costs and Contestability

Definition of Sunk Costs: Costs that cannot be recovered upon exit
(e.g., bespoke machinery, R&D, branding).

Relationship: The lower the sunk costs, the more contestable the market.
The higher the sunk costs, the less contestable the market (firms fear
entering if exit is expensive).

6\. Exam Focus: Key Takeaways

Define contestability and distinguish it from competition.

Explain the four key characteristics of a contestable market.

Analyse how the threat of \"hit and run\" competition influences firm
behaviour (e.g., limit pricing).

Explain the role of sunk costs as a key determinant of contestability.

Identify and explain different types of barriers to entry/exit.
