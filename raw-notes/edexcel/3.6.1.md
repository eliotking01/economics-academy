Theme 3.6.1: Government Intervention

1\. Intervention to Control Monopoly Power

The **Competition and Markets Authority (CMA)** aims to promote
competition and protect consumers.

a\) Controlling Mergers

Prevents mergers/acquisitions that would create a firm with \>25% market
share or **substantially lessen competition** (e.g., lead to higher
prices, less choice).

Can block mergers or force the sale of assets.

b\) Regulating Existing Monopolies (especially Natural Monopolies)\
*(Hand-draw Diagram: Price Regulation in a Natural Monopoly)*

Draw a natural monopoly diagram: **LRAC falls** over the relevant output
range. Draw **MC** below AC. Draw **AR** and **MR**.

Mark the unregulated monopoly outcome: Qm, Pm (where MC=MR).

Price Cap (RPI-X): Impose a maximum price (Preg) below Pm.

At Preg, the firm must produce **Qreg** (where AR = Preg).

This **lowers price** and **increases output** towards the allocatively
efficient level (where P=MC).

The firm must cut costs (X) to maintain profit.

|  |  |  |
|----|----|----|
| Method | How it Works | Evaluation |
| Price Regulation (RPI-X) | Sets a maximum price. Firm must limit price increases to inflation (RPI) minus an efficiency gain (X). | **+** Lowers prices, raises output. **–** Hard to set 'X', may reduce investment. |
| Profit Regulation | Limits the **rate of return** (profit) a monopoly can earn. | **+** Curbs excessive profits. **–** No incentive to cut costs ('gold-plating'), inefficiency. |
| Quality Standards / Performance Targets | Mandates minimum service levels (e.g., train punctuality). | **+** Prevents quality cutting. **–** Increases costs, monitoring is difficult. |

2\. Intervention to Promote Competition & Contestability

Policies to make markets more competitive and easier for new firms to
enter (contestable).

- Promotion of Small Businesses: Grants, tax breaks, advice.

- **Deregulation:** Removing **barriers to entry** (e.g., licensing
  laws) to increase contestability.

- **Competitive Tendering:** Government contracts are put out to **open
  tender**, forcing public and private firms to compete.

- **Privatisation:** Selling state-owned assets to the private sector.
  Aims to increase **efficiency** through profit incentive and
  competition. (Can be evaluated: may lead to higher prices if a private
  monopoly replaces a state one).

3\. Intervention to Protect Suppliers & Employees

a\) Protecting Suppliers from Monopsony Power

- **Problem:** A dominant buyer (e.g., supermarket) can force down
  prices, harming suppliers.

- Solutions: CMA investigations, minimum price floors, strengthening
  supplier legal rights, encouraging codes of practice.

b\) Protecting Employees

- National Minimum Wage (NMW): Legally enforced wage floor. (See
  previous diagram).

- Employment Legislation: Laws on health & safety, maximum working
  hours, unfair dismissal.

- **Support for Trade Unions:** Allows collective bargaining to balance
  employer power.

4\. Exam Focus: Key Takeaways

- **Explain** the role of the **CMA** in controlling mergers and
  regulating monopolies.

- Draw and analyse the effect of price regulation (RPI-X) on a natural
  monopoly.

- **Evaluate** different methods of monopoly regulation (price caps vs.
  profit regulation).

- Analyse policies to increase competition/contestability (deregulation,
  privatisation).

- **Explain** government intervention to **protect suppliers** (vs.
  monopsony) and **employees** (NMW, legislation).
