Theme 3.4.5: Monopoly

1\. Key Characteristics

**Single seller / dominant firm** (in the UK, defined as \>25% market
share).

**High barriers to entry** (legal, patents, economies of scale,
ownership of key resources).

**Price maker** with significant **market power**.

No close substitutes for the product.

2\. Profit Maximisation & Diagram

A monopoly maximises profit where Marginal Cost (MC) = Marginal Revenue
(MR).

\*(Hand-draw Diagram 1: Monopoly Supernormal Profit)\*

Draw axes: Cost/Revenue (£) vs Quantity (Q).

Draw downward-sloping AR (Demand) and steeper MR curves.

Draw the **MC** and **AC** curves.

Find **Qm** where **MC = MR**.

From Qm, go up to the AR curve to find the monopoly price **Pm**.

From Qm, go up to the AC curve to find the average cost **Cm**.

Shade the area of **supernormal profit**: **(Pm - Cm) × Qm**.

**Note:** Unlike competitive markets, supernormal profit can be
sustained **in the long run** due to barriers to entry.

3\. Third-Degree Price Discrimination

Charging different prices to different consumer groups for the same
product to increase revenue.

Conditions Required:

1.  **Market power** (price-setting ability).

2.  **Different PED** in different sub-markets (e.g., business vs.
    leisure travellers).

3.  **Ability to separate markets** and prevent resale (arbitrage).

\*(Hand-draw Diagram 2: Third-Degree Price Discrimination)\*

Draw **two sets of axes side-by-side** (Sub-market A and Sub-market B).

In each, draw a downward-sloping **AR (Demand)** and **MR** curve. Make
Sub-market A's demand more **inelastic** (steeper).

Draw the same **MC curve** in both (horizontal for simplicity).

In each sub-market, find where **MC = MR** to determine output (Qa, Qb).

From these outputs, find the different prices on the AR curves
(**Pa** in the inelastic market, **Pb** in the elastic market). **Pa \>
Pb**.

Result: The monopoly charges a higher price in the market with more
inelastic demand, increasing total profit compared to a single price.

4\. Costs & Benefits of Monopoly

|  |  |  |
|----|----|----|
| Stakeholder | Potential Benefits | Potential Costs (Drawbacks) |
| Firm | **Supernormal profit** for reinvestment (dynamic efficiency). **Economies of scale** lower AC. | **X-inefficiency** due to lack of competition. |
| Consumers | **Possible lower prices** if economies of scale are passed on. Innovation from R&D. | **Higher prices**, **lower output** (P \> MC). **Reduced choice** and potentially poorer quality. **Allocative inefficiency**. |
| Economy | May be **internationally competitive** due to size. Can invest in risky R&D. | Loss of consumer surplus. Misallocation of resources. Inequitable (regressive). |

5\. Natural Monopoly

Occurs when one firm can supply the entire market at a lower average
cost than two or more firms due to huge economies of scale and high
fixed costs (e.g., water, rail networks, energy grids).

LRAC falls over the entire range of market demand. Competition
is inefficient.

**Government regulation** (e.g., **price caps**, RPI-X) is often needed
to prevent exploitation.
