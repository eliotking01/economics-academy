Theme 3.4.4: Oligopoly

1\. Key Characteristics

**High concentration ratio** – a few large firms dominate the market
(e.g., 5-firm ratio \> 60%).

**High barriers to entry and exit** (e.g., high start-up costs, sunk
costs, economies of scale).

**Interdependence of firms** – each firm<span dir="rtl">’</span>s
decisions affect others and vice versa. This is the **core feature**.

Differentiated products (usually).

2\. Concentration Ratios

A measure of market concentration. E.g., a **5-firm concentration ratio
of 80%** means the top 5 firms have 80% of the market.

High ratio = more concentrated/less competitive market.

**Calculation:** (Total sales of top 'n' firms / Total market sales) ×
100.

3\. Collusive vs. Non-Collusive Behaviour

Due to **interdependence**, firms must choose whether
to **collude** (cooperate) or **compete**.

- **Collusion:** Firms agree (formally or informally) to restrict
  competition.

  - **Overt Collusion:** Formal agreement (e.g., **cartel** like
    OPEC). **Illegal.**

  - **Tacit Collusion:** Informal understanding (e.g., **price
    leadership** – one firm sets price, others follow).

  - **Aim:** Act like a **monopoly** to raise price, restrict output,
    and earn **supernormal profit**.

- **Non-Collusive Behaviour:** Firms actively compete, often
  using **non-price competition**.

4\. Game Theory & The Prisoner's Dilemma

Game theory analyses strategic decision-making
under **interdependence**.

(Hand-draw Diagram: A Payoff Matrix – Prisoner's Dilemma)

Create a 2x2 grid. Label rows/columns with two firms' choices
(e.g., **High Price / Low Price**).

In each box, show the **payoff** (profit) for each firm based on the
combined choice.

Identify the **dominant strategy** (the best move regardless of the
rival's choice). Often, this is to **cheat** on collusion (e.g., lower
price).

Identify the **Nash Equilibrium** (the outcome where no firm can improve
its position by changing strategy alone). This is often a **worse
outcome for both** than if they had colluded.

**Insight:** Even when collusion is mutually beneficial, the **incentive
to cheat** for short-term gain can lead to a competitive outcome.

5\. Types of Competition

Price Competition:

**Price War:** Repeated price cuts to undercut rivals – hurts all firms'
profits.

**Predatory Pricing:** Temporarily pricing below cost to drive out a
competitor – often illegal.

**Limit Pricing:** Setting price low enough to deter new entrants.

Non-Price Competition (Very Common in Oligopoly):

Advertising & branding

Loyalty schemes

Product differentiation & innovation

Customer service, warranties, packaging

6\. The Kinked Demand Curve Model (for Non-Collusive Oligopoly)

(Note: Concept implied, but not explicitly requested. It's a key
oligopoly model in many specs, so including a brief note is prudent for
a student's general knowledge.)

**Assumption:** If a firm **raises price**, rivals **won't follow** (so
it loses many customers – demand is **elastic**). If it **lowers
price**, rivals **will match** (so it gains few customers – demand
is **inelastic**).

**Result:** A **kinked demand curve** at the current price, leading
to **price rigidity** and a **discontinuous MR curve**.
