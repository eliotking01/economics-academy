Theme 4.2.1: Absolute and Relative Poverty

1\. Key Definitions

**Absolute Poverty:** Exists when individuals **cannot afford the basic
necessities** for survival (food, water, shelter, healthcare). Measured
against a fixed international threshold (e.g., living on less than
\$1.90 a day). Primarily an issue in **developing countries**.

**Relative Poverty:** Exists when household income is **below a certain
proportion of median income** in that society (e.g., below 60% of median
household income in the UK). It measures **inequality** and social
exclusion. The main concern in **developed countries**.

2\. Causes of Changes in Poverty

A. Factors Reducing Absolute Poverty:

Sustained Economic Growth: Raises average incomes.

**Effective Government Intervention:** Targeted welfare benefits, access
to education/healthcare.

**Globalisation & Trade:** Can create jobs and increase incomes in
developing economies.

B. Factors Influencing Relative Poverty:

**Changes in Income Distribution:** Worsens if the incomes of the rich
grow faster than the poor.

**Changes in Asset Prices:** Rising house prices can **reduce** relative
poverty for homeowners but **increase** it for renters.

**Government Tax & Benefit Policies:** Progressive systems reduce it;
austerity or benefit cuts can increase it.

**Labour Market Changes:** Growth of low-paid, insecure work
(**precariat**) can increase it.

3\. The Relationship Between Growth and Poverty

**Absolute Poverty:** Strong evidence that **economic growth is the most
powerful driver** of reducing absolute poverty globally (e.g., in China,
India).

**Relative Poverty:** Growth alone does not guarantee a reduction. If
growth benefits are **unequally distributed**, relative poverty
can **increase even as absolute poverty falls**.

Key Exam Tips:

- **Crucial Distinction:** You **must** be able to distinguish
  between **absolute** (a fixed subsistence line) and **relative** (a
  moving line based on societal norms) poverty.

- **Measurement:** Know how each is measured. Relative poverty is
  a **measure of inequality** within a country.

- Evaluation:

  - Reducing absolute poverty is widely seen as a priority, but methods
    (e.g., rapid industrialisation) may have environmental/social costs.

  - Reducing relative poverty often involves **redistributive
    policies** (taxes, benefits) which can create **trade-offs** with
    incentives and economic efficiency.

- **UK Context:** Relative poverty is the relevant measure for UK
  policy. Discuss causes like wage stagnation, benefit changes, and
  regional disparities.
