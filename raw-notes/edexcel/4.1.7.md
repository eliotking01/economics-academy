Theme 4.1.7: Balance of Payments (BoP)

1\. Structure of the Balance of Payments

The BoP records all financial transactions between a country and the
rest of the world. It must always balance.

**Current Account:** Records **trade in goods/services, primary
income** (investment profits, remittances), and **secondary
income** (transfers like aid).

**Capital Account:** Records minor capital transfers (e.g., debt
forgiveness).

**Financial Account:** Records **flows of investment** (e.g., FDI,
portfolio investment) and changes in **foreign exchange reserves**.

2\. Current Account: Deficits and Surpluses

- A **Current Account Deficit** occurs when outflows (imports, income
  paid abroad) \> inflows (exports, income received). The country is
  a **net borrower**.

- A **Current Account Surplus** occurs when inflows \> outflows. The
  country is a **net lender**.

- By identity, a current account deficit must be financed by a **surplus
  on the financial/capital account** (e.g., selling assets to or
  borrowing from foreigners).

3\. Causes of a Current Account Deficit

**Low Competitiveness:** Higher relative inflation or lower productivity
than trading partners.

**Strong Exchange Rate:** An overvalued currency makes exports expensive
and imports cheap.

**Strong Domestic Demand:** High consumer spending sucks in imports
(high income elasticity of demand for imports).

**Non-Price Factors:** Poor quality, design, or marketing of domestic
goods.

**Structural Factors:** Reliance on imported raw materials or finished
goods (deindustrialisation).

4\. Policies to Reduce a Current Account Deficit

**Expenditure-Switching:** Policies to shift spending from imports to
domestic goods (e.g., **depreciation** of the
currency, **protectionism**). Risk: retaliation.

**Expenditure-Reducing:** **Deflationary fiscal/monetary policy** to
reduce aggregate demand and thus import spending. Risk: higher
unemployment, lower growth.

**Supply-Side Policies:** Aim to improve **long-run
competitiveness** (e.g., investment in skills, infrastructure,
innovation). These work in the long term but are slow and costly.

5\. Significance of Global Imbalances

**Persistent Deficits:** Can lead to rising **foreign debt**, loss of
asset ownership, and vulnerability to sudden stops in capital flows
(e.g., Greek debt crisis).

**Persistent Surpluses:** Can indicate an economy overly reliant on
external demand, suppressing domestic living standards, and causing
global trade tensions (e.g., China-US trade relations).

**Global Imbalance:** Large, persistent imbalances between major
economies (e.g., US deficit, Chinese/German surplus) are a source of
global financial instability.

Key Exam Tips:

**Know the Components:** Be able to list and briefly explain the parts
of the **current account** and **financial account**.

**Link the Accounts:** Explain that a current account deficit
is **financed by** a financial account surplus (capital inflows).

**Policy Evaluation:** This is a key evaluation area. No policy is
cost-free. **Expenditure-reducing** policies harm
growth; **expenditure-switching** (depreciation) may cause
inflation; **supply-side** policies are slow. The "do nothing" option
relies on a floating exchange rate.

**Context Matters:** A deficit is not always bad if it
finances **productive investment** that boosts future growth and export
capacity. It is **persistent, structural deficits** that are
problematic.
