Theme 3.6.2: The Impact of Government Intervention

1\. Desired Outcomes of Intervention

Governments intervene in markets (e.g., through regulation, taxation,
subsidies) to correct market failures and improve outcomes for society.
Key aims include:

Lower Prices & Greater Affordability

Higher Product Quality & Safety Standards

Greater Consumer Choice

**Increased Efficiency** in resource allocation

**Controlled Monopoly Profits** (ensuring only normal profit in
regulated industries)

Protection of Workers, Consumers, and the Environment

2\. Limits and Problems with Intervention

Intervention can fail, leading to **government failure**, where the
outcome is worse than the original market failure.

A. Regulatory Capture

**Definition:** When the regulated firms gain excessive influence over
the regulators, shaping the rules in their own favour rather than the
public interest.

**Causes:** Intensive lobbying, the promise of future jobs for
regulators in the industry ("revolving door"), and the complexity of
regulation giving firms an information advantage.

**Result:** Regulation becomes ineffective and serves to protect
incumbent firms, potentially raising prices and reducing competition.

B. Asymmetric Information

**Problem:** The government/regulator often has **less
information** than the firms they are trying to regulate. This makes
effective, well-targeted intervention very difficult.

**Result:** Poorly designed policies that are easily circumvented,
impose excessive costs, or have unintended negative consequences.

C. Other Limitations:

**Administrative Costs:** The cost of implementing and enforcing
regulation can exceed the social benefits gained.

**Unintended Consequences:** Policies can distort incentives (e.g.,
creating black markets, reducing innovation).

**Conflicting Objectives:** A policy to achieve one aim (e.g., low
prices via a price cap) may worsen another (e.g., reducing investment
and future quality).

Key Exam Tips:

**Evaluation is Key:** A common question asks you to *evaluate* the
effectiveness of government intervention. Your answer **must** weigh the
potential **benefits** (correcting market failure) against the **risks
and limitations** (government failure, regulatory capture, etc.).

**Real-World Examples:** Use specific cases. For **regulatory capture**,
you could reference lobbying in the energy or finance sectors.
For **asymmetric information**, think of complex markets like
derivatives or pharmaceuticals.

**Balanced Conclusion:** The possibility of government failure doesn't
mean governments should never intervene, but that interventions must be
carefully designed, transparent, and subject to review. Sometimes,
a **market-based solution** (e.g., taxation over a ban) may be less
prone to failure.
