Theme 2.2.5: Net Trade (X-M)

1\. Definition

Net trade is **exports minus imports (X-M)**, a key component of
Aggregate Demand. A **surplus** (X\>M) adds to AD, while
a **deficit** (X\<M) subtracts from it.

2\. Key Influences on Net Trade

A. Real Incomes

**Rising UK real incomes:** Increases **demand for imports** as
consumers buy more goods, including from abroad. This worsens the trade
balance.

**Rising real incomes abroad:** Increases **demand for UK exports**.
This improves the trade balance.

B. Exchange Rates

- Appreciation of Pound Sterling:

  - UK exports become **more expensive** for foreigners → exports fall.

  - Imports become **cheaper** for UK consumers → imports rise.

  - **Likely worsens** the trade balance (X-M falls).

- Depreciation of Pound Sterling:

  - UK exports become **cheaper** for foreigners → exports rise.

  - Imports become **more expensive** for UK consumers → imports fall.

  - **Likely improves** the trade balance (X-M rises).

C. State of the World Economy

**Strong global growth:** Boosts **demand for UK exports**, improving
the trade balance.

**Global recession:** Reduces **demand for UK exports**, worsening the
trade balance.

D. Protectionism

**Increase in protectionism** (e.g., tariffs, quotas) makes imports more
expensive/restricted, potentially reducing them. However, it often leads
to **retaliation**, harming exports. The net effect is uncertain but
often reduces overall trade volumes.

E. Non-Price Factors (Competitiveness)

- Quality, design, reliability, and marketing of UK goods relative to
  foreign alternatives. Strong non-price competitiveness boosts exports.

3\. Critical Analysis: The Marshall-Lerner Condition & J-Curve

The impact of an exchange rate change depends on **price elasticity of
demand (PED)**.

**Marshall-Lerner Condition:** A depreciation will **only improve** the
trade balance if the **PED of exports + PED of imports \> 1**. If demand
is inelastic, a fall in price leads to less revenue.

**The J-Curve Effect:** In the **short run**, demand is often inelastic
(contracts are fixed, consumers take time to switch). A depreciation may
therefore **worsen** the trade balance initially (the downward dip of
the 'J'), before improving it in the **longer run** as demand becomes
more elastic (the upward slope of the 'J').

Key Exam Tips:

**Direction of Change:** Use the AD formula. An **increase** in net
trade (a larger surplus or smaller deficit) **increases AD**. A decrease
in net trade **reduces AD**.

**Primary Influence:** For the UK, **changes in real income** (domestic
and global) are often the most significant short-term driver of the
trade balance.

**Evaluation:** Always consider **time lags** and **elasticities** when
analysing exchange rate impacts (the J-Curve). The effect is not
instantaneous.

**Link to Objectives:** A persistent trade deficit can conflict with the
objective of a **balanced current account**. Policies to fix it (e.g.,
depreciation) may conflict with **low inflation** (as imports become
dearer).
