Theme 1.2.9: Indirect Taxes & Subsidies

1\. Indirect Taxes

**Definition:** A tax levied on **goods and services** (e.g., VAT,
excise duties). It is paid **indirectly** by the consumer via the
producer.

**Purpose:** To **raise government revenue** and/or to **discourage
consumption** of demerit goods (goods with negative externalities, e.g.,
cigarettes, sugar).

Impact on Supply: An indirect tax increases a
firm<span dir="rtl">’</span>s costs of production. This is shown by
a leftward (upward) shift of the supply curve.

2\. Analysing a Specific (Per-Unit) Tax

\*(Hand-draw Diagram 1: Show a market in equilibrium at P1, Q1 (S1=D).
Draw a new supply curve, S2, perfectly parallel and above S1 by the
amount of the tax. Label the new consumer price (Pc), the new producer
price (Pp), and the new quantity (Q2).)\*

- **The Shift:** Supply shifts left from **S1** → **S2**. The **vertical
  distance** between S1 and S2 equals the **tax per unit**.

- New Equilibrium:

  - Consumer Price Rises (from P1 to Pc).

  - Quantity Falls (from Q1 to Q2).

  - Producer Price Falls (from P1 to Pp). Pp = Pc – tax.

- Tax Incidence (Who Pays?):

  - **Consumer Incidence:** (Pc – P1) × Q2. The portion of the tax paid
    by consumers via the higher price.

  - **Producer Incidence:** (P1 – Pp) × Q2. The portion of the tax paid
    by producers via lower revenue.

  - **Government Revenue:** Total Tax × Q2 = (Pc – Pp) × Q2. The sum of
    consumer and producer incidence.

3\. The Role of Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

The relative burden (incidence) of a tax depends on the PED of the
product.

\*(Hand-draw Diagram 2a & 2b: Draw two sets of axes. In 2a, show a steep
(inelastic) demand curve. In 2b, show a flat (elastic) demand curve. In
both, impose the same tax (parallel S shift).)\*

|  |  |
|----|----|
| **Demand is Price INELASTIC** (e.g., Cigarettes, Petrol) | **Demand is Price ELASTIC** (e.g., Restaurant meals, Branded pizza) |
| Consumers pay most of the tax (large consumer incidence). | Producers pay most of the tax (large producer incidence). |
| **Qd falls only slightly.** Tax is effective at raising revenue, less effective at reducing consumption. | **Qd falls significantly.** Tax is more effective at reducing consumption, but raises less revenue and hurts producers more. |
| **Reason:** Consumers are less responsive to price changes. | **Reason:** Consumers are highly responsive to price changes. |

4\. Subsidies

**Definition:** A **per-unit payment** from the government to producers
to **lower their costs**.

**Purpose:** To **encourage production/consumption** of merit goods
(goods with positive externalities, e.g., solar panels, education) or to
support key industries.

Impact on Supply: A subsidy lowers a firm<span dir="rtl">’</span>s costs
of production. This is shown by a rightward (downward) shift of the
supply curve.

5\. Analysing a Subsidy

\*(Hand-draw Diagram 3: Show a market at equilibrium P1, Q1 (S1=D). Draw
a new supply curve, S2, perfectly parallel and below S1 by the amount of
the subsidy. Label the new consumer price (Pc), the new producer price
(Pp), and the new quantity (Q2).)\*

- **The Shift:** Supply shifts right from **S1** → **S2**.
  The **vertical distance** between S1 and S2 equals the **subsidy per
  unit**.

- New Equilibrium:

  - Consumer Price Falls (from P1 to Pc).

  - Quantity Rises (from Q1 to Q2).

  - Producer Price Rises (from P1 to Pp). Pp = Pc + subsidy.

- Subsidy Incidence (Who Benefits?):

  - **Consumer Incidence:** (P1 – Pc) × Q2. The benefit to consumers
    from the lower price.

  - **Producer Incidence:** (Pp – P1) × Q2. The benefit to producers
    from higher revenue per unit.

  - **Government Cost:** Total Subsidy × Q2 = (Pp – Pc) × Q2. The total
    expenditure by the government.

6\. Exam Focus: Key Takeaways

Draw accurate diagrams for both a specific tax and a subsidy, labeling
all key prices, quantities, and areas of incidence/government
revenue/cost.

**Explain** that taxes shift supply **left**, subsidies shift
supply **right**.

**Analyse** the impact on consumers, producers, and the government.

**Critically evaluate** the effectiveness of a tax/subsidy by
applying **PED.** This is a crucial evaluation point. A tax on an
inelastic good raises more revenue but reduces consumption less.

**Understand** that the policy aims (e.g., reduce consumption vs. raise
revenue) determine whether a tax is effective.
