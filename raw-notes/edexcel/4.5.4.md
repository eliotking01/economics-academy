Theme 4.5.4: Macroeconomic Policies in a Global Context

1\. Global Constraints on National Policy

Due to globalisation and interdependence, national governments face
significant constraints:

**Capital Mobility:** Policies (especially interest rate changes) can
trigger large capital flows, affecting exchange rates and financial
stability.

**Competitiveness:** National tax or regulation policies can cause firms
to relocate.

**Policy Spillovers:** One country's policies (e.g., US quantitative
easing, Chinese subsidies) affect others.

**Limited Sovereignty:** Membership of trading blocs (EU) or monetary
unions (Eurozone) surrenders key policy tools.

2\. Policy Responses to Common Global Aims

A. Reducing Fiscal Deficits/Debt:

**Approaches:** Austerity (spending cuts/tax rises) vs. Growth-oriented
consolidation (reform with investment).

**Global Context:** Markets (lenders) impose discipline. High debt can
lead to loss of confidence and capital flight.

B. Reducing Poverty & Inequality:

**Approaches:** Vary by ideology—progressive taxation and social
spending vs. market-led growth.

**Global Context:** Tax competition between countries can limit
progressive taxation. Global corporate tax agreements (e.g., OECD
minimum tax) attempt to overcome this.

C. Managing Interest Rates & Money Supply:

**Dilemma:** Central banks must consider **domestic
inflation/growth** AND **exchange rate stability**. Raising rates to
support the currency can choke domestic demand.

**Global Context:** Synchronised global monetary policy (e.g., post-2008
easing, post-2021 tightening) is often necessary but can create asset
bubbles globally.

D. Improving International Competitiveness:

**Approaches:** Supply-side policies (investment), currency
depreciation, or protectionism.

**Global Context:** "Beggar-thy-neighbour" policies (competitive
devaluation, tariffs) risk **retaliation and trade wars**, harming all.

3\. Controlling Global Companies (MNCs/TNCs)

**Challenges:** MNCs exploit **transfer pricing** to shift profits to
low-tax jurisdictions, engage in **regulatory arbitrage**, and wield
significant political power.

**Policy Responses:** Require international cooperation—strengthened
global tax rules, harmonised regulations, and support for developing
countries to build institutional capacity to negotiate and enforce
agreements.

4\. Problems Facing Policymakers

**Inaccurate/Outdated Information:** Data lags make real-time policy
difficult.

**Uncertainties & External Shocks:** Pandemics, wars, and financial
crises are unpredictable and limit policy control.

**Conflicting Objectives & Trade-offs:** e.g., austerity reduces debt
but may increase poverty; protectionism may save jobs but reduces
competitiveness long-term.

**Political Constraints:** Short-term electoral cycles discourage
long-term, economically optimal policies.

Key Exam Tips:

**The Key Theme is Constraint:** Globalisation means **no country is an
island**. Effective policy requires awareness of, and often coordination
with, other nations.

**Evaluation of Policy Effectiveness:** Always ask: "How might global
factors limit or alter the outcome of this policy?" (e.g., a UK interest
rate cut might cause a damaging currency crash).

**Case Studies:** Use **post-2008 austerity** (constrained by market
fears), **post-COVID inflation** (a global supply shock requiring a
coordinated monetary response), and **global tax deals** as examples of
policy in a global context.

**Conclusion:** National policymakers have less autonomy than textbook
models suggest. Success often depends on **international
cooperation** and navigating complex **trade-offs**.
