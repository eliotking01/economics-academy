Theme 4.1.4: Terms of Trade

1\. Definition and Calculation

The **Terms of Trade (ToT)** measures the **relative price** of a
country's exports compared to its imports.

**Formula**: Terms of Trade Index = (Index of Average Export Prices /
Index of Average Import Prices) x 100

**Interpretation:** A rise in the index (e.g., from 100 to 105) is
an **improvement** – each unit of exports buys more imports. A fall is
a **deterioration**.

2\. Factors Influencing the Terms of Trade

**Relative Inflation Rates:** Higher inflation than trading partners
tends to **increase export prices**, potentially improving the ToT (if
demand is inelastic).

**Relative Productivity Growth:** Faster productivity growth can **lower
export costs/prices**, potentially worsening the ToT.

**Changes in Exchange Rates:** An **appreciation** makes exports more
expensive and imports cheaper, tending to **improve** the ToT.

**Changes in Global Commodity Prices:** For primary product exporters
(e.g., oil), a price boom sharply improves their ToT.

**Changes in Demand:** Rising global demand for a country's exports
increases their price, improving the ToT.

3\. Impact of Changes in the Terms of Trade

The effect depends crucially on the **price elasticity of demand
(PED)** for exports and imports.

An *Improvement* in ToT (Export Prices Rise / Import Prices Fall):

**Potential Benefit:** Each export buys more imports, potentially
raising **living standards**.

**Potential Cost:** If export demand is **elastic**, higher export
prices cause a large fall in quantity sold, **reducing export
revenue** and worsening the **current account** and **GDP**.

A *Deterioration* in ToT (Export Prices Fall / Import Prices Rise):

**Potential Cost:** More exports are needed to buy the same imports,
lowering living standards.

**Potential Benefit:** If export demand is **elastic**, lower export
prices cause a large rise in quantity sold, **increasing export
revenue**, **GDP**, and **employment** (the **Marshall-Lerner
condition**).

Key Exam Tips:

**PED is Crucial:** Never state that an improvement/deterioration is
simply 'good' or 'bad'. **Always** consider the **elasticity of
demand**.

**Link to Current Account:** The ToT and the trade balance are related
but different. An improved ToT can coincide with a worsening current
account if export revenue falls.

**Real-World Application:** Use commodity exporters (e.g., an oil
producer) as a clear example. An oil price boom **dramatically
improves** their ToT and boosts national income.

**Evaluation:** The impact of a ToT change on an economy's welfare
is **ambiguous**. It depends on the **cause** of the change and
the **underlying elasticities**.
