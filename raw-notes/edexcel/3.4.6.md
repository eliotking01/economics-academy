Theme 3.4.6: Monopsony

1\. What is a Monopsony?

A market with a **single buyer** (or a **dominant buyer**) of a good,
service, or factor of production (e.g., labour).

**Examples:** A large supermarket chain buying from farmers, the NHS
hiring nurses, a major manufacturer in a small town.

2\. Key Characteristics

**Wage/Price Maker:** The buyer has the power to set the price it pays
(e.g., the wage rate).

**Profit Maximiser:** Aims to minimise its costs. It recognises that
buying more will **increase the market price** it has to pay
for *all* units.

3\. The Monopsony Diagram (in a Labour Market)

A monopsonist faces an **upward-sloping supply curve of labour (ACL)**.
To hire more workers, it must offer a higher wage to attract them, and
this higher wage must be paid to **all workers**, not just the new one.

(Hand-draw Diagram: The Monopsony Labour Market)

Draw axes: Wage Rate (£) vs Quantity of Labour (Q).

Draw an upward-sloping Supply of Labour (S = ACL) curve.

Draw a **Marginal Cost of Labour (MCL)** curve **above and steeper than
the Supply curve**. This is because hiring one more worker raises the
wage for all previous workers.

Draw the firm's **Marginal Revenue Product (MRP)** curve (its demand for
labour, downward sloping).

The profit-maximising monopsonist hires where **MCL = MRP**. Mark this
quantity **Qm**.

To attract Qm workers, it pays the wage from the **Supply curve** at Qm.
Mark this wage **Wm**.

The competitive market equilibrium would be where Supply (ACL) = MRP at
a higher wage (Wc) and higher quantity (Qc).

4\. The Outcome of Monopsony Power

**Lower Wage (Wm \< Wc):** The monopsonist **depresses the wage** (or
price paid to suppliers) below the competitive level.

Lower Employment/Quantity (Qm \< Qc): It employs/purchases less than in
a competitive market.

Exploitation: Workers/suppliers receive a wage/price (Wm) that is less
than their marginal revenue product (MRP at Qm). This gap is the degree
of monopsonistic exploitation.

5\. Costs & Benefits of Monopsony

|  |  |  |
|----|----|----|
| For the Monopsonist (Buyer) | For Suppliers/Workers | For Consumers & Society |
| **Benefit:** Lower costs, higher profits. | **Cost:** Lower income/wages. | **Potential Short-run Benefit:** **Lower consumer prices** if cost savings are passed on. |
|  | **Cost:** Fewer jobs/outlets for goods. | **Major Long-run Cost:** **Market failure.** **Under-utilisation of resources** (e.g., unemployment, farm closures). Lower wages can reduce quality and supply in the long run (e.g., teacher/nurse shortages). |

6\. Government Intervention

To counter monopsony power, governments may:

- Introduce/strengthen **minimum wages** (can raise employment in a
  monopsony market up to a point).

- Empower **trade unions** to negotiate collectively.

- Use **competition policy** to regulate the buying power of large
  firms.
