Theme 2.3.1: Aggregate Supply (AS)

1\. Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS)

**Definition:** The total planned output of goods/services in an economy
at a given price level in a time period where **at least one factor of
production is fixed** (e.g., capital stock, technology).

2\. The SRAS Curve

\*(Hand-draw Diagram 1: The SRAS Curve)\*

Draw axes: Y-axis = Average Price Level, X-axis = Real GDP.

Draw an upward-sloping SRAS curve.

**Why is the SRAS curve upward sloping?**\
In the short run, as output increases, firms face **rising unit
costs** (e.g., paying overtime wages, using less efficient machinery).
To maintain profitability, they need to charge **higher prices** for
this increased output.

3\. Movements vs. Shifts of the SRAS Curve

- A Movement Along the SRAS Curve:

  - **Cause:** A change in the **Average Price Level** (ceteris
    paribus).

  - This shows firms changing their **level of output** in response to a
    changing price level.

\*(Hand-draw Diagram 2: Movement Along SRAS)\*\
On your SRAS curve, show a movement **up and right** labelled "Extension
of SRAS" and a movement **down and left** labelled "Contraction of
SRAS".

- A Shift of the Entire SRAS Curve:

  - **Cause:** A change in **any condition of supply** (a change in the
    costs of production for firms across the economy).

  - This changes the level of output firms are willing to supply at
    every given price level.

\*(Hand-draw Diagram 3: Shift of SRAS)\*\
Draw an initial SRAS1 curve. Draw a new curve to the right
labelled **SRAS2 (Increase in AS)**. Draw another to the left
labelled **SRAS3 (Decrease in AS)**.

4\. Determinants of SRAS (Causes of Shifts)

Any factor that changes the **costs of production** for firms will shift
SRAS.

5\. Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS)

**Definition:** The total potential output of an economy when all
factors of production are fully and efficiently employed. It represents
the economy's **productive capacity**.

**Key Idea:** The LRAS curve is **independent of the price level**. It
is determined by the **quantity and quality** of factors of production
(resources, labour, capital, technology, entrepreneurship).

\*(Hand-draw Diagram 4: The LRAS Curve)\*\
On new axes (Price Level vs Real GDP), draw a **vertical line**. Label
it **LRAS**. The position of this line represents the economy's maximum
sustainable output (full employment output).

6\. The Relationship Between SRAS and LRAS

- **SRAS** shows what firms *will* produce at different price levels in
  the short run.

- **LRAS** shows what the economy *can* produce at its maximum
  potential.

- **Long-Run Economic Growth** is shown by an **outward shift of the
  LRAS curve** (an increase in productive capacity). This is analogous
  to an outward shift of the PPF.

- In the long run, the economy will tend towards the LRAS level of
  output. SRAS shocks are temporary adjustments around this long-run
  trend.

7\. Exam Focus: Key Takeaways

**Draw** the upward-sloping SRAS and vertical LRAS curves.

**Explain** the slope of the SRAS curve (rising unit costs).

**Distinguish** between a **movement along** SRAS (price change) and
a **shift** of SRAS (change in costs of production).

**Analyse** how specific events (e.g., oil price rise, currency
depreciation, a new subsidy) shift the SRAS curve.

**Understand** that LRAS represents the economy's **productive
potential** and that shifts in LRAS signify **long-term economic
growth**.
