Theme 2.3.3: Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS)

1\. What is LRAS?

Definition: The total potential output of an economy when all factors of
production are fully and efficiently employed. It represents the
economy's productive capacity.

**Key Idea:** Shifts in LRAS represent **long-term economic growth** (or
decline), equivalent to an outward (or inward) shift of the PPF.

2\. Two Views of the LRAS Curve

Economists disagree on the shape of the LRAS curve, leading to two main
models.

a\) The Classical View

**Belief:** In the long run, the economy will **always adjust to full
employment** of resources via flexible prices and wages.

**The Curve:** LRAS is **perfectly inelastic (vertical)** at the level
of output corresponding to full employment (**Yfe**).

\*(Hand-draw Diagram 1: Classical LRAS)\*

Draw AD/AS axes (Price Level vs Real GDP).

Draw a **vertical line** labelled **LRAS (Classical)** at output
level **Yfe**.

Show an AD curve intersecting it at equilibrium **P1, Yfe**.

**Analysis:** Any change in AD only changes the **price level** in the
long run, **not the level of real output**. The economy self-corrects
back to Yfe.

b\) The Keynesian View

**Belief:** The economy can be **stuck in equilibrium below full
employment** due to low confidence ('animal spirits') and wage/price
stickiness. Government intervention is often needed.

The Curve: LRAS is non-linear (L-shaped).

**Perfectly elastic (horizontal)** at low output: Vast spare capacity
means output can rise without inflation.

**Upward sloping** as the economy approaches capacity: Rising output
causes some inflation.

Perfectly inelastic (vertical) at full capacity output (Yfe).

\*(Hand-draw Diagram 2: Keynesian LRAS)\*

Draw AD/AS axes.

Draw a three-section LRAS curve: a horizontal section, a curving upward
section, and a final vertical section at **Yfe**. Label it **LRAS
(Keynesian)**.

Show an AD curve intersecting in the horizontal section, illustrating an
economy in a recession with high unemployment.

**Analysis:** In a recession, increases in AD can raise **real output
without causing inflation**. This justifies expansionary fiscal/monetary
policy.

3\. Factors Influencing LRAS (Causes of Shifts)

Any change in the **quantity or quality** of the factors of production
(land, labour, capital, enterprise) will shift the LRAS curve, changing
the economy's productive potential.

\*(Hand-draw Diagram 3: Shifts in LRAS)\*\
Draw a vertical Classical LRAS1 curve. Draw a new vertical curve to the
right labelled **LRAS2 (Economic Growth)**. Draw another to the left
labelled **LRAS3 (Economic Decline)**.

|  |  |  |
|----|----|----|
| Factor | Effect on LRAS (Outward Shift) | Example |
| Technological Advances | ↑ **Quality of Capital** → More efficient production. | AI, automation, new manufacturing techniques. |
| Changes in Education & Skills | ↑ Quality of Labour (Human Capital) → More productive workforce. | Increased spending on training and higher education. |
| Changes in Productivity | ↑ **Output per worker/per hour** → More can be produced with the same inputs. | Better management practices, process innovation. |
| Demographic Changes & Migration | ↑ **Quantity of Labour** → Larger working-age population. | Positive net migration, rising birth rate. |
| Increase in Capital Stock | ↑ **Quantity of Capital** → More/better machinery and infrastructure. | High levels of investment by firms and government. |
| Competition Policy & Deregulation | ↑ **Efficiency/Enterprise** → More dynamic markets encourage innovation. | Breaking up monopolies, reducing barriers to entry. |
| Institutional Improvements | ↑ **Quality of Institutions** → Better legal frameworks encourage investment. | Stronger property rights, less corruption. |

4\. Exam Focus: Key Takeaways

Draw and explain the Classical (vertical) and Keynesian (L-shaped) LRAS
curves.

**Understand the critical policy difference:** Classical economists
believe the economy self-corrects; Keynesians believe active government
intervention is often necessary.

**Identify** factors that cause **outward shifts in LRAS** (long-run
economic growth).

**Analyse** how economic growth is shown: an **outward shift of
LRAS** (and the PPF).

**Evaluate** which LRAS view is more appropriate in different economic
contexts (e.g., a deep recession vs. a period of steady growth).
