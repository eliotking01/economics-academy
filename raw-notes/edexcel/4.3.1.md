Theme 4.3.1: Measures of Development

1\. Economic Growth vs. Economic Development

**Economic Growth:** A **narrower** concept, measured by increases
in **real GDP**.

**Economic Development:** A **broader** concept, meaning **sustainable
improvements** in living standards, freedom, and welfare. It includes
non-material factors like health, education, and equality.

2\. Single vs. Composite Indicators

**Single Indicators:** Measure one aspect (e.g., infant mortality rate,
literacy rate, access to clean water). Useful for specific comparisons
but give an incomplete picture.

**Composite Indicators:** Combine several single indicators into one
index to give a more rounded view of development.

3\. The Human Development Index (HDI)

The main composite measure, created by the UN. It combines:

**Health:** Life expectancy at birth.

**Education:** Mean years of schooling and expected years of schooling.

**Income:** Real GNI per capita at **PPP** (to account for cost of
living).

Each element is equally weighted. Countries are scored from 0 (lowest)
to 1 (highest).

Advantages of HDI:

- Provides a **broader picture** than GDP alone.

- Allows for meaningful international comparisons.

- Highlights that investment in health and education is as important as
  income.

Limitations of HDI:

- **Does not measure inequality** within a country (uses averages).

- **Does not account for environmental sustainability** or quality of
  life factors (e.g., freedom, pollution).

- Relies on data that can be **unreliable or out-of-date** for some
  countries.

4\. Other Development Indicators

**Inequality-adjusted HDI (IHDI):** Adjusts the HDI
score **downwards** to account for inequality in health, education, and
income. Shows the **loss** in potential human development.

**Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI):** Measures **acute poverty** by
examining deprivations across health, education, and living standards at
the **household level**. Identifies *who* is poor and *how* they are
poor.

**Quality of Life Indicators:** e.g., happiness indices, measures of
political freedom, environmental quality.

Key Exam Tips:

**Critical Distinction:** Always distinguish
between **growth** (quantitative, GDP) and **development** (qualitative,
welfare). Development requires growth, but growth does not automatically
lead to development.

**HDI Evaluation:** This is a classic evaluation topic. The HDI
is **better than GDP per capita** for measuring living standards, but it
is **still limited** (ignores inequality, sustainability, etc.).

**Use of Indicators:** Different indicators are useful for different
purposes. The **MPI** is best for targeting poverty relief, while
the **HDI** is best for broad international league tables.

**Link to Policies:** Governments aiming to improve their HDI must focus
on **policies that increase life
expectancy** (healthcare), **educational attainment** (schooling),
and **income** (economic growth with equity).
