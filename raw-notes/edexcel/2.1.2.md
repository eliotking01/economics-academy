Theme 2.1.2: Inflation

1\. Definitions: Inflation, Deflation, Disinflation

**Inflation:** A **sustained increase** in the general/average price
level of goods and services in an economy.

**Deflation:** A **sustained decrease** in the general price level
(negative inflation rate).

**Disinflation:** A **decrease in the rate of inflation** (prices are
still rising, but at a slower pace).

2\. Measuring Inflation: The Consumer Price Index (CPI)

- **CPI** is the UK's official measure. The Bank of England has an
  inflation target of **CPI 2%**.

- How it's calculated:

  **Basket of Goods:** A representative 'basket' of ~700 goods/services
  an average household buys is created (updated annually).

  **Weighting:** Items are weighted based on their **proportion of
  household spending** (e.g., housing has a higher weight than
  clothing).

  **Price Collection:** Prices are collected monthly from numerous
  locations.

  Index Calculation:

  - Price of basket in current year / Price of basket in base year × 100
    = **CPI**.

  - **Inflation Rate** = \[(CPI Year 2 – CPI Year 1) / CPI Year 1\] ×
    100.

3\. Limitations of the CPI

**Not Representative:** The 'average' basket may not reflect spending
patterns of different households (e.g., pensioners vs. students).

**Regional Variations:** Ignores differences in price changes across the
country.

**Quality Changes:** Fails to fully account for improvements in product
quality over time.

**Substitution Bias:** Slow to reflect when consumers switch to cheaper
alternatives.

**Data Collection Errors:** Based on a sample survey, which can be
inaccurate.

4\. Retail Price Index (RPI)

- An alternative measure that **includes housing costs** (e.g., mortgage
  interest, council tax).

- Generally yields a **higher inflation rate** than CPI.

- Considered less reliable for international comparisons and
  is **not** the UK's official target measure.

5\. Causes of Inflation

a\) Demand-Pull Inflation

**Cause:** Aggregate Demand (AD) rises **faster** than Aggregate Supply
(AS). "Too much money chasing too few goods."

**Triggers:** ↑Consumer Confidence, ↑Govt Spending, ↓Taxes, ↓Interest
Rates, ↑Export Demand.

b\) Cost-Push Inflation

**Cause:** A rise in the **costs of production** for firms, shifting the
AS curve leftwards.

**Triggers:** ↑Global Commodity Prices, ↑Wages, ↑Business Taxes, Supply
Chain Shocks.

c\) Growth of the Money Supply

**Link:** Excessive growth in the money supply (e.g., via Quantitative
Easing) can fuel **demand-pull inflation**.

d\) Wage-Price Spiral

- A **vicious cycle** where rising prices lead to higher wage demands,
  which increase costs for firms, leading to further price rises (a mix
  of demand-pull and cost-push).

6\. Effects of Inflation

|  |  |
|----|----|
| Stakeholder | Potential Negative Effects |
| Consumers | ↓ **Purchasing power** of income. ↓ Real value of savings. Menu costs (inconvenience). Uncertainty. |
| Firms | Uncertainty discourages **investment**. Erodes **international competitiveness** (if higher than trading partners). Menu costs of changing prices. |
| Government | May need to increase **state pension/benefit payments**. Higher inflation can lead to political dissatisfaction. |
| Workers | ↓ Real wages if nominal wage rises \< inflation. Can lead to **industrial disputes** (demand for higher pay). |
| Economy | Can cause **balance of payments** problems. High/variable inflation distorts price signals, leading to **misallocation of resources**. |
