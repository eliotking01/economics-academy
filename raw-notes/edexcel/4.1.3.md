Theme 4.1.3: Pattern of Trade

1\. What is the Pattern of Trade?

The **pattern of trade** refers to the **changing composition and
direction** of a country's exports and imports. It
shows **what** goods/services are traded and **with which countries**.

2\. Factors Influencing the Pattern of Trade

A. Comparative Advantage

Countries specialise in producing goods/services where they have
a **lower relative opportunity cost**.

This is the fundamental economic driver of trade, leading to **long-term
specialisation** (e.g., China in manufacturing, Saudi Arabia in oil).

B. Growth of Emerging Economies

The rapid industrialisation of countries like **China, India, and
Vietnam** has dramatically shifted global trade.

They have become major exporters of manufactured goods and importers of
raw materials and luxury goods, altering global supply chains.

C. Trading Blocs and Bilateral Agreements

The formation of blocs (e.g., EU, USMCA) creates **trade
creation** (more trade between members) but also **trade
diversion** (away from non-members), reshaping regional trade flows.

D. Relative Exchange Rates

An **appreciation** makes exports more expensive and imports cheaper,
potentially worsening the trade balance with certain partners.

A **depreciation** has the opposite effect, boosting exports to
price-sensitive markets.

E. Relative Inflation Rates

If a country's inflation is persistently higher than its trading
partners', its goods become **less price-competitive**, reducing exports
and increasing imports.

F. Non-Price Factors

- **Quality, design, and innovation** can determine trade patterns
  independent of price (e.g., German engineering, Japanese electronics).

G. Government Policy

- **Protectionism** (tariffs, quotas) or **trade
  liberalisation** directly alter which goods are traded and with whom.

Key Exam Tips:

- **Dynamic, Not Static:** Emphasise that trade patterns
  are **constantly evolving** due to the factors above. Use the shift in
  UK trade from the Commonwealth to the EU/US as a key example.

- **Link to Globalisation:** The changing pattern of trade is a **core
  feature of globalisation** (e.g., the rise of global supply chains
  centred on Asia).

- **Analysis:** When asked about a change, identify the most relevant
  factor(s). For example, a loss of market share might be due
  to **increased competition from emerging economies** (comparative
  advantage) or an **uncompetitive exchange rate**.

- **Impact:** Changing trade patterns can lead to **structural
  change** within economies (e.g., deindustrialisation in the West) and
  create **winners and losers** among industries and workers.
