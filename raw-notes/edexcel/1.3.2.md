Theme 1.3.2: Externalities

1\. Key Definitions

**Externality:** A cost (negative) or benefit (positive) that affects
a **third party** not directly involved in the economic transaction.
Also called a **spillover effect**.

**Private Costs/Benefits:** The costs incurred or benefits received by
the **producers or consumers directly involved** in the transaction.

Social Costs/Benefits: The total impact on society.

Social Cost (SC) = Private Cost (PC) + External Cost

Social Benefit (SB) = Private Benefit (PB) + External Benefit

**Market Failure:** Occurs when the free market fails to allocate
resources efficiently, leading to a **welfare loss**. Externalities are
a core cause of market failure.

2\. Negative Externalities of Production

- **What it is:** The **production** of a good imposes **external
  costs** on third parties (e.g., pollution, noise, congestion).

- **Example:** Coal-fired electricity generation creates air pollution
  (external cost) harming public health.

- **The Problem:** Producers only consider their **private costs
  (MPC)**. They ignore external costs, so goods
  are **over-produced** and **under-priced** compared to what is
  socially optimal.

Mark the **social optimum** where MSC = MSB (**Qopt, Popt**).

3\. Positive Externalities of Consumption

- **What it is:** The **consumption** of a good provides **external
  benefits** to third parties.

- **Example:** Education benefits the individual (private benefit) but
  also creates a more productive workforce and lower crime rates for
  society (external benefit). These are **merit goods**.

- **The Problem:** Consumers only consider their **private benefits
  (MPB)**. They ignore external benefits, so merit goods
  are **under-consumed** and **under-produced** compared to what is
  socially optimal.

\*(Hand-draw Diagram 2: Positive Externality of Consumption)\*

Draw standard axes. Label Supply as **MSC** (Marginal Social Cost).

Draw the **MPB** (Marginal Private Benefit) curve.

Draw the **MSB** (Marginal Social Benefit) curve **above and parallel to

Mark the market equilibrium where MPB = MSC (Qm, Pm).

- **Diagram Analysis:** The market consumes at **Qm \< Qopt**. The
  shaded triangle represents the welfare loss from under-consumption. To
  reach the social optimum, output and consumption must **increase**.

4\. Government Intervention to Correct Externalities

For Negative Externalities (to reduce output to Qopt):

**Indirect Taxation:** Shifts the MPC curve up towards the MSC,
internalising the external cost (e.g., carbon tax).

**Regulation & Legislation:** Enforce limits on pollution (e.g.,
emissions standards).

**Tradable Pollution Permits:** Create a market for pollution rights.

**For Positive Externalities** (to increase output to Qopt):

**Subsidies:** Shift the MPC curve down, lowering the cost to
producers/consumers (e.g., subsidies for renewable energy or training).

**Government Provision:** Directly provide the good/service (e.g., state
education, public parks).

**Information Campaigns:** Shift the MPB curve up by increasing
awareness of benefits.

5\. Exam Focus: Key Takeaways

- **Define** key terms: externality, social/private cost/benefit,
  welfare loss.

- **Draw, label, and explain** the two core externality

- **Identify** the welfare loss area and explain what it represents.

- **Analyse** the difference between the market equilibrium (Qm) and the
  social optimum (Qopt).

- **Evaluate** different government interventions for correcting
  externalities, considering their effectiveness, cost, and potential
  unintended consequences.
