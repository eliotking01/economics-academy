Theme 4.5.3: Public Sector Finances

1\. Key Definitions and Distinctions

Fiscal (Budget) Deficit: When government spending (G) \> tax revenue
(T) in a single year.

**National Debt:** The **accumulated total** of all past government
borrowing (past deficits minus surpluses).

**Automatic Stabilisers:** **Non-discretionary** changes to spending and
revenue that automatically dampen the economic cycle (e.g., welfare
payments rise in a recession, tax revenue falls).

**Discretionary Fiscal Policy:** **Deliberate** changes to
spending/taxation by the government to manage Aggregate Demand (AD).

**Cyclical Deficit:** The part of a deficit caused by the **economic
cycle** (e.g., a recession). It should correct as the economy recovers.

**Structural Deficit:** The part of a deficit that exists even at
the **peak of the economic cycle** (at full employment). It indicates an
underlying imbalance between permanent spending and revenue.

2\. Factors Influencing the Size of Deficits and Debt

State of the Economy (Cyclical): The biggest influence. Deficits widen
automatically in recessions (lower T, higher G) and narrow in booms.

**Government Policy (Discretionary):** Decisions to increase spending or
cut taxes will increase the deficit, and vice versa.

**Demographic Changes:** An ageing population increases structural
spending on pensions and healthcare.

**Unforeseen Events:** Wars, pandemics, or financial crises lead to
large, unexpected increases in spending.

Interest Rates: Higher rates increase the cost of servicing existing
debt, adding to annual spending.

3\. Significance and Consequences

**Debt Servicing Costs:** High debt requires large interest payments,
creating a significant **opportunity cost** (funds not available for
public services or tax cuts).

**Inter-generational Equity:** Current borrowing may pass a **financial
burden** to future taxpayers.

**Crowding Out:** Government borrowing can **increase interest rates**,
making it harder for private firms to invest, potentially reducing
long-term growth.

**Fiscal Flexibility:** High debt limits the government's ability to
use **discretionary fiscal policy** to fight future recessions.

**Market Confidence & Credit Rating:** If debt is seen as unsustainable,
lenders may demand **higher interest rates** or refuse to lend,
triggering a crisis. A downgraded credit rating increases borrowing
costs.

Key Exam Tips:

- **Automatic vs. Discretionary:** This is a crucial
  distinction. **Automatic stabilisers** help smooth the cycle without
  new laws. **Discretionary policy** is an active choice.

- **Cyclical vs. Structural:** A key evaluation point. A large deficit
  during a deep recession is mostly **cyclical** and less concerning. A
  large deficit during a boom is **structural** and a serious policy
  problem.

- **Evaluation of High Debt:** There is no magic "safe" level. The
  impact depends on:

  - **What the borrowing financed** (e.g., productive investment vs.
    current consumption).

  - **The growth rate of the economy** (if GDP grows faster than debt,
    the debt-to-GDP ratio falls).

  - **Interest rates** (low rates make high debt more sustainable).

- **Policy Trade-offs:** Policies to reduce a deficit
  (**austerity**—cutting G or raising T) can **reduce AD and slow
  growth** in the short term, creating a painful trade-off.
