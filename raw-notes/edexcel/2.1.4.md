Theme 2.1.4: The Balance of Payments

1\. Structure of the Balance of Payments

The Balance of Payments (BoP) is a record of all financial transactions
between a country and the rest of the world. It must always balance. Its
two main components are:

**Current Account:** Records trade in goods/services, income flows, and
current transfers.

**Capital & Financial Account:** Records flows of investment (e.g.,
buying foreign companies/stocks) and changes in reserves.

2\. The Current Account

This is the most-watched part of the BoP and has four main elements:

**Trade in Goods (Visible Balance):** Exports and imports of physical
items (e.g., cars, machinery). **Exports are a credit (+), imports are a
debit (-).**

**Trade in Services (Invisible Balance):** Exports and imports of
services (e.g., banking, tourism, insurance).

**Primary Income:** Net income from investments abroad (profits,
interest, dividends) and remittances from workers overseas.

**Secondary Income (Transfers):** Net transfers of money without a
direct quid pro quo (e.g., EU contributions, foreign aid).

Key Balances:

**Balance of Trade in Goods & Services:** (Goods Exports - Goods
Imports) + (Services Exports - Services Imports).

**Current Account Balance:** The sum of all four elements above. It is
often expressed as a **percentage of GDP** for comparison.

3\. Deficits and Surpluses

- A **Current Account Deficit** occurs when outflows (debits) exceed
  inflows (credits). This means the country is a net borrower from the
  rest of the world.

- A **Current Account Surplus** occurs when inflows exceed outflows.
  This means the country is a net lender to the rest of the world.

- Persistent large deficits or surpluses can indicate underlying
  economic issues and are a key government policy target.

4\. Causes of a Current Account Deficit

**Strong Domestic Demand:** High consumer spending sucks in imports.

**Lack of International Competitiveness:** High relative inflation, poor
quality, or an overvalued exchange rate make exports expensive and
imports cheap.

**Non-Price Factors:** Poor marketing, supply-side weaknesses, or
protectionism abroad.

**Structural Factors:** Deindustrialisation leading to reliance on
imported manufactured goods.

5\. Interconnectedness and Trade-offs

- **Global Interdependence:** Economies are deeply linked through
  complex supply chains. A shock in one country (e.g., a war, pandemic)
  disrupts trade globally.

- **Policy Trade-offs:** Correcting a current account deficit can
  conflict with other macroeconomic objectives.

  - **Example:** Using higher interest rates to improve the deficit (by
    strengthening the exchange rate) could reduce aggregate demand,
    investment, and economic growth, potentially raising unemployment.

Key Exam Tips:

**Know the Components:** Be able to break down the current account into
its four parts and explain what each contains.

**Analyse Causes:** For a given scenario, identify whether a deficit is
caused by **demand-side** (high spending) or **supply-side** (lack of
competitiveness) factors.

**Evaluation:** A current account deficit is not inherently bad—it may
finance valuable investment. The key is whether it is **persistent and
structural** vs. **temporary and cyclical**. Also, consider if policies
to fix it cause worse problems (e.g., recession).

**Link to Exchange Rates:** The exchange rate is a primary mechanism for
adjusting the current account (a depreciation makes exports cheaper and
imports dearer).
