Theme 2.1.3: Employment and Unemployment

1\. Key Definitions

Unemployed: A person of working age who is without work, available to
start work within two weeks, and has actively sought work in the last
four weeks (ILO definition).

**Labour Force:** The sum of those in employment and those unemployed
(actively seeking work).

**Economically Inactive:** People of working age who are **not seeking
work** (e.g., full-time students, early retirees, long-term sick,
discouraged workers).

2\. Measuring Unemployment in the UK

Two main methods are used:

**ILO/Labour Force Survey (LFS):** The **internationally
comparable** measure. A household survey based on the ILO criteria
above.

**Claimant Count:** Measures the number of people **claiming
unemployment-related benefits** (e.g., Universal Credit). It is
usually **lower** than the LFS measure as not all unemployed people
claim benefits.

3\. Types and Causes of Unemployment

**Structural:** Caused by a **long-term decline** in an industry or
a **mismatch of skills** (e.g., decline of UK coal mining, automation).
Requires retraining.

**Cyclical (Demand-Deficient):** Caused by a **fall in aggregate
demand** during an economic recession. Firms lay off workers as output
falls.

**Frictional:** **Short-term** unemployment when people are **between
jobs** or entering the labour market. Inevitable in a dynamic economy.

**Seasonal:** Unemployment due to **time of year** (e.g., tourism,
agriculture).

**Real Wage (Classical):** Caused by wages being **above the market
equilibrium** (e.g., due to strong unions, high minimum wages), creating
an excess supply of labour.

4\. Underemployment

Occurs when workers are in jobs that **underutilise their skills** or
when they **want to work more hours** than they currently do. It
represents a **waste of productive potential** and is often hidden in
employment statistics.

5\. The Impact of Unemployment

**On Individuals:** Loss of income, skills atrophy, poverty, health
problems, and social isolation.

**On the Economy:** A **waste of scarce resources** (labour), leading to
a loss of potential output (a negative output gap) and lower GDP.

**On the Government:** Increased spending on welfare benefits and lost
tax revenue (income tax, VAT), worsening the **government budget**.

**On Firms:** Lower consumer spending reduces sales and profits. A
larger pool of labour may reduce wage pressures.

6\. The Impact of Migration on the Labour Market

**On Employment:** Immigration **increases the size of the labour
force**, helping to fill skill shortages and vacancies, potentially
increasing total output (GDP).

**On Wages:** Theory suggests an increased supply of labour could **put
downward pressure on wages**, particularly for low-skilled roles.
Evidence on the scale of this effect is mixed.

**On Unemployment:** Immigrants may compete for some jobs, but they also
create demand through their own spending, which can create new jobs.
The **net effect** on the unemployment rate is generally considered to
be small.

Key Exam Tips:

**Crucial Distinction:** Know the difference
between **unemployment** (actively seeking work) and **economic
inactivity** (not seeking work). A fall in unemployment can be
misleading if people become discouraged and leave the labour force.

**Categorise Causes:** Be able to identify the **type of
unemployment** from a scenario. This dictates the appropriate policy
response (e.g., demand-side policies for cyclical, supply-side for
structural).

**Evaluation:** Unemployment statistics have limitations—they don't
capture underemployment, the hidden economy, or regional variations.
The **Claimant Count vs. LFS** is a common evaluation point.

**Link to Macroeconomics:** Connect unemployment to **output
gaps** (cyclical unemployment signifies a negative output gap) and
government **fiscal policy** (higher unemployment increases benefit
spending).
