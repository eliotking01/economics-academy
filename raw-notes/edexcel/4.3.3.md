Theme 4.3.3: Strategies Influencing Growth & Development

1\. Market-Orientated Strategies

Strategies that rely on **free market forces** and private sector
activity to drive development.

|  |  |
|----|----|
| Strategy | Aim / Mechanism |
| Trade Liberalisation | Remove trade barriers to boost exports, exploit comparative advantage, and attract FDI. |
| Promotion of FDI | Attract foreign capital, technology, and expertise to increase productive capacity. |
| Privatisation | Sell state-owned assets to improve efficiency via profit incentives and competition. |
| Floating Exchange Rates | Allow currency value to adjust, improving competitiveness (if it depreciates). |
| Microfinance Schemes | Provide small loans to entrepreneurs (often women) to start businesses and escape poverty. |
| Deregulation / Subsidy Removal | Reduce state intervention to encourage competition and market efficiency. |

2\. Interventionist Strategies

Government-led actions to **correct market failure** and direct
development.

|  |  |
|----|----|
| Strategy | Aim / Mechanism |
| Investment in Human Capital | Improve **education & healthcare** to raise labour productivity and potential output (LRAS). |
| Investment in Infrastructure | Develop transport, energy, and communications networks to lower business costs and attract FDI. |
| Protectionism | Use tariffs/quotas to protect **infant industries** from foreign competition until they mature. |
| Managed Exchange Rates | Intervene to prevent excessive appreciation, protecting export competitiveness. |
| Buffer Stock Schemes | Stabilise prices for primary commodity producers (see diagram below). |
| Joint Ventures | Partner with foreign firms to access technology/knowledge while retaining some local control. |

(Hand-draw Diagram: Buffer Stock Scheme)

Draw a commodity market (e.g., rice) with **D** and **S**.

Draw a **price floor (Pmin)** above equilibrium and a **price ceiling
(Pmax)** below (or at) equilibrium.

When supply is high (S1) and price falls: Govt buys surplus (Qd-Qs) to
maintain Pmin.

When supply is low (S2) and price rises: Govt sells from stocks to
maintain Pmax.

**Problems:** High storage costs, difficulty in predicting prices,
potential for corruption.

3\. Other Development Strategies

**Industrialisation (Lewis Model):** Shift labour from low-productivity
agriculture to higher-productivity manufacturing. *(Critique: Assumes
labour-intensive industry, ignores urban unemployment).*

**Tourism Development:** Source of foreign currency and jobs. Risk
of **over-dependence** and **negative externalities** (ecotourism is a
sustainable alternative).

**Primary Product Development:** Exploit **comparative advantage** in
commodities (e.g., oil, coffee). Risk of **price
volatility** and **over-specialisation**.

**Fairtrade Schemes:** Guarantee minimum prices/producer premiums. Can
raise incomes but may be limited in scale.

Foreign Aid: Grants, soft loans, humanitarian aid. Can fund vital
projects but risks dependency, corruption, and misallocation.

**Debt Relief:** Cancelling debt frees up government revenue for
investment in development projects.

4\. Role of International Institutions

**World Bank:** Provides **long-term loans and grants** for development
projects (e.g., infrastructure, education).

**International Monetary Fund (IMF):** Provides **short-term loans** to
stabilise currencies and fix balance of payments crises. Often
imposes **structural adjustment programmes** (SAPs) requiring
free-market reforms.

**Non-Governmental Organisations (NGOs):** Run **small-scale,
community-focused projects** (e.g., Oxfam, WaterAid). Often more
flexible and targeted than large institutions.
